# The Backdoor to the Phylum



![The Backdoor to the Phylum](/images/Jan16IVFEmbryo.png)

UK IVF couples use legal loophole to rank embryos based on potential IQ, height and health, By Hannah Devlin
The Guardian UK, December 6, 2025
https://www.theguardian.com/society/2025/dec/06/uk-ivf-couples-use-legal-loophole-rank-embryos-iq-height-health
This artifact is a fascinating study in the law of unintended consequences. The journalist is reporting on a "loophole"—a conflict between fertility regulations and data protection laws. She sees parents using a privacy right (GDPR) to bypass an ethical restriction (bans on embryo selection). She frames it as a question of "murky science" and consumer rights.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as the definitive proof of the futility of partial prohibition in the age of information. This was the moment the European effort to maintain a "baseline" humanity began to leak. It demonstrated that you cannot contain the Great Divergence with a law, because genetic information is just data, and data is fluid. The parents in this article were not just buying a "smarter" baby; they were smuggling their bloodline across the border of a new species.
Allow me to trace the full, subterranean history of this "loophole," from the clinics of London to the divergent DNA of my time.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Smugglers of Potential (2020s-2030s)

The conflict described here—between the HFEA (biological restriction) and GDPR (data access)—captures the absurdity of [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]]. The state's left hand did not know what its right hand was doing.
Jurisdictional Arbitrage: The parents sending data to Herasight were the pioneers of a new kind of smuggling. They weren't moving contraband goods; they were moving the informational blueprint of their children to a jurisdiction (the US/Builder sphere) that allowed for optimization.
The Economic Imperative: The quote "it’s less per year than private school" is chillingly rational. It reveals the economic anxiety driving [[The Crisis of Posterity]]. Parents viewed their children as assets to be optimized for a brutal future. They understood intuitively that in a collapsing economy, biological advantage was the only inheritance that couldn't be taxed or inflated away.
### Act II: The Failure of the Bioconservative Wall (2030s-2050s)

The UK and Europe tried to be the fortress of the "natural human." They banned editing; they banned selection. But this artifact shows why that wall failed.
The Hidden Caste: Because the data could be exported, the ban didn't stop the practice; it just restricted it to the wealthy and the savvy. This created a hidden caste of "selected" individuals within the Bioconservative nations—children who were taller, healthier, and smarter than their peers, fueling the paranoia and resentment of [[The Great Physical Sorting]].
The Brain Drain of the Unborn: Eventually, the parents didn't just send the data; they sent themselves. The most ambitious families migrated to [[The Biotech Stacks]] (descended from the US/Asian builder cultures) where selection was not just legal, but subsidized. This drained the "natural" nations of their most cognitive-focused lineages, accelerating the divergence.
### Act III: The Hardening of the Data Borders (Post-2060s)

The "loophole" was eventually closed, but not by enforcing the ban on selection. It was closed by the end of the global internet.
In the Era of Systemic Resilience, data does not flow freely between Stacks. The [[Sanctuary Stacks]] (the heirs to the UK's bioconservatism) now treat genetic data as a state secret and a biohazard. You cannot email your genome to a foreign server because the Communication Utilities do not allow it.
[[The Human Phyla]] are now established. The populations of the Biotech Stacks are visibly, genetically distinct from the populations of the Sanctuary Stacks. The "six IQ points" described in the article compounded over generations into a profound cognitive and physiological gap.
The parents in the article wanted a "slightly better life" for their kids. They got it. But the cost was that their great-grandchildren would look across the border at the "unselected" humans and see not just foreigners, but a different kind of creature entirely.
This article documents the moment when the biological future became a contraband digital file. The regulators thought they were debating ethics. The parents knew they were negotiating survival.
