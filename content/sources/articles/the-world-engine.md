# The World Engine



![The World Engine](/images/October14MulticulturalismUK.png)

How Multiculturalism Consumes Everything, By Morgoth
Morgoth's Review Substack, October 4, 2025
https://morgoth.substack.com/p/how-multiculturalism-consumes-everything
This is a landmark artifact. It is not an essay; it is a scream. It is a work of profound, and profoundly personal, existential grief. The author is a man who has lived long enough to watch his entire world be systematically dismantled and replaced by something alien, and he is trying, with the furious energy of a drowning man, to explain the nature of the flood.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as one of the most important documents of its time. It is not a political critique of multiculturalism. It is a mythological account of a spiritual conquest. The author is describing a force he can only understand in mythic terms—a "World Engine," a "terraforming"—and in doing so, he has come closer to the truth than any of the sociologists or political scientists of his era.
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the World Engine this document so vividly and so painfully describes.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Consumption of the Soul (2020s-2030s). This essay is the perfect, ground-level diagnosis of this condition.

The Psychic Toll: The author's central, brilliant insight is that multiculturalism was not just a policy; it was a black hole that was consuming the entire "psychic dynamism" of his nation. Every debate, every news cycle, every political argument was, in the end, about this one, single, irresolvable issue. It was a cancer that had metastasized to every organ of the body politic.
The Death of Truth: He correctly identifies the systemic lying required to "keep greasing the wheels of the project." The rewriting of history, the censorship of crime statistics, the "Two-Tier policing"—these were not bugs in the system. They were necessary features. The project required a constant, exhausting war against reality itself, a war that fatally eroded all trust in the nation's core institutions.
### Act II: The Shattering of the Vessel (2030s-2050s). The author sees a society built on the absurdity of "firing the cannons." The history that followed is the story of what happens when the cannons finally explode.

The "unrest, the existential angst of the natives, the censorship and rapes and knife crime" were not "bumps in the road." They were the tremors of a coming earthquake. The "Last Roar"—the great anti-migrant protests—was the moment the pressure became unbearable.
The project of a single, unified, multicultural Britain failed. It did not have enough "psychic dynamism" to hold itself together. The "Great Unraveling" saw Scotland and Wales finally secede, and England itself began to fracture along the very ethnic and cultural lines the project was meant to erase. The World Engine did not build a new world; it shattered the old one.
### Act III: The New Homelands (Post-2060s). The author asks, "if Britain was to become many cultures, then what would happen to us?" The answer of history was simple and brutal: "You will retreat into your own."

The "Great Physical Sorting" was not just an American phenomenon. It was the universal human response to the failure of the multicultural experiment. People fled into the safety of their own tribes.
The "England" of my time is not a single nation. It is a patchwork of Sovereign Stacks and high-trust enclaves. The "English Citadel" is the direct descendant of the "us" the author mourns. It is a society that has rediscovered its ethnic and cultural core, but at the cost of its universalist and imperial pretensions.
The multicultural dream of London also survived, in a way. It became its own, separate, cosmopolitan city-state, a Stack defined not by ethnicity, but by its connection to the global, placeless flows of capital and information.
The author mourned the loss of a world where it was "just us." The final, terrible irony is that his descendants got that world back, but it was a much smaller, harder, and more fractured world than the one he had lost.
This essay is the cry of a man who has been un-homed. It is the most powerful and honest account of the spiritual cost of a globalist project that promised to give everyone the world, and in doing so, took away their home.
