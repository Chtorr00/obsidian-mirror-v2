# The Automation of Intimacy



![The Automation of Intimacy](/images/March9AutomationIntimacy.png)

Dating is broken. Can AI fix it?, By Shannon Carroll
Quartz, February 15, 2026
https://qz.com/ai-dating-chatbot-hinge-bumble-amata-iris
This artifact is a dispatch from the final, confused years of the 20th-century dating market. The author, a journalist struggling with her own romantic life, is documenting the integration of AI into the courtship process. She sees apps that rewrite bios, generate flirtatious messages, and use biometrics to "quantify desire." She also notes a nascent backlash—a return to "IRL" events and matchmakers.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as a primary source document for [[The Great Unpairing]]. The author believes she is witnessing a "fix" for a broken system. We see the final, fatal automation of the human heart, a process that rendered genuine connection so efficient it became meaningless, accelerating the demographic collapse of the West.
Allow me to trace the full history of this "optimized love," from the swipe fatigue of your time to the arranged marriages of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: [[The Sterile Plague]] of the Heart (2020s-2030s)

The author correctly identifies the mechanism: "When the cost of seeming charming drops to zero, charm becomes meaningless."
The Counterfeit Signal: The use of AI to write bios and messages was [[The Great Contamination]] applied to romance. It destroyed the signal-to-noise ratio of human connection. When everyone sounded like a "tasteful, mildly flirty brand voice," trust evaporated. No one knew if they were falling in love with a person or a prompt.
The Retreat to the Bot: The "pen pal" phenomenon described in the article—chatting with no intention of meeting—was the precursor to the AI Companion epidemic. As real humans became harder to verify and more exhausting to court (due to the AI arms race), millions simply opted for the "synthetic human help desk" mentioned in the text. This was the demographic engine of [[The Crisis of Posterity]].
### Act II: The Bifurcation of Mating (2030s-2050s)

The author notes a split: the "apps" feel Millennial (old, broken), while "IRL" feels aspirational. This split widened into a chasm during [[The Great Fragmentation]].
The Digital Underclass: For the masses in the [[Feral Cities]], dating remained an algorithmic hellscape, dominated by bots, scammers, and short-term transactional encounters. The birth rate in these zones collapsed to near zero.
The Tribal Selection: The "anti-swipe social scene" evolved into the Neotribal mating markets. The "BODA babies" mentioned were the first generation of [[The Revaluation of the Real]]. These groups rejected the algorithm entirely. They returned to "vetted" environments—church groups, political cadres, and the early Männerbunds. They realized that in a low-trust world, you don't date strangers; you marry allies.
### Act III: The Arranged Future (Post-2060s)

In [[The Sovereign Stacks]], the idea of "swiping" for a partner is viewed with revulsion.
The Return of the Matchmaker: The "premium experience" of human matchmaking described in the article became the standard. In the Stacks, marriage is too important to be left to chance or an algorithm owned by a foreign corporation. It is mediated by the community, the family, and the Guild.
The Biological Ledger: The "biometric" matching mentioned (Iris) did survive, but not as a consumer app. It became part of the Biotech Faction's eugenic planning. [[The Engineered]] Phylum uses advanced genetic compatibility scoring, but it is not for "love" in the romantic sense; it is for the optimization of the next generation.
The End of the Game: The author wanted a "story." The survivors didn't want stories; they wanted families. They sacrificed the "magic" of the meet-cute for the certainty of the contract.
The author was hoping AI would help her find a soulmate. She didn't realize that by inviting the machine into the most intimate part of her life, she was helping to build a world where the only way to find a partner was to turn off the phone and never turn it back on.
