# The Paralysis of the Gavel



![The Paralysis of the Gavel](/images/December15AIasLegislator.png)

Congress Has Forgotten How to Legislate, By Michael S. Kochin
American Greatness, November 14, 2025
https://amgreatness.com/2025/11/14/congress-has-forgotten-how-to-legislate/
This artifact is a work of profound institutional nostalgia and acute diagnostic clarity. The author, writing for a conservative publication, is looking at the central nervous system of the American republic—the United States Congress—and finding it dead. He is asking for a return to the era of the "builder-legislator," the Carl Vinsons who could see a thirty-year problem and engineer a thirty-year solution.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this document not as a call to action, but as a coroner’s report. The author correctly identifies that the legislative muscle of the American state had atrophied. What he could not see was that this paralysis was not a temporary bug; it was the terminal condition of [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]]. The machine had stopped because the consensus required to fuel it had evaporated.
Allow me to trace the history of the vacuum this author identified, from the empty chamber of your time to the automated governance of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
The Theater of the State (2020s-2030s)
The author’s observation that Congress had become a venue for "performance" rather than governance is the key. In the final years of the old order, the legislative branch ceased to be a place where laws were written and became a soundstage for the Culture Wars Endgame.
The Incentive Structure: [[The Shattered Focus]] of the electorate meant that complex, boring, "Vinson-style" legislation went unnoticed, while "two-minute viral video clips" generated political capital. The system naturally selected for performers, not architects.
The Abdication: The author pleads for laws on AI and the FDA. He didn’t get them. Because Congress could not legislate, power flowed like water to the path of least resistance: the executive decree and, more dangerously, [[The Parasovereigns]] (tech giants and corporate cartels). The "void" the author describes was filled by private actors who wrote the rules of the AI age in their own terms of service, leading directly to the unchecked acceleration that hit [[The AI Energy Wall]].
The Veto of the Real (2030s-2050s)
The author yearned for a "21st-century industrial capacity." History delivered it, but not through a bill passed in Washington.
The Collapse of the Consensus: To pass a "Two-Ocean Navy Act," you need a unified nation willing to pay for it. By the mid-30s, [[The Great Dissolution]] and [[The Involuntary Jubilee]] had rendered the federal purse empty and the national will fractured. There was no "we" left to build a navy for.
The Legislature of Physics: When human legislators failed to address the critical infrastructure problems (FAA, grid, supply chains), reality stepped in. [[The Supply Chain Wars]] and the infrastructure collapses of [[The Great Fragmentation]] acted as a brutal form of legislation. The laws of thermodynamics and logistics imposed the constraints that Congress refused to enact. The "boring, essential work" was eventually done, but it was done by local emergency committees and warlords trying to keep the lights on, not by senators in suits.
The Rise of Protocol (Post-2060s)
The author asked, "Who is a legislator?" In the Era of Systemic Resilience, the answer is rarely a human being.
[[Protocol as Law]]: [[The Sovereign Stacks]] that rose from the ruin of the US did not rebuild Congress. They viewed the human legislature as a single point of failure, prone to corruption and gridlock. Instead, they adopted Protocol as Law. The rules of the market, the grid, and the social contract are encoded into [[The AI Governance Layer]].
The End of Ambiguity: Vinson’s laws were subject to interpretation. The protocols of the Stacks are self-executing. The "new framework" for AI liability that the author wanted is now hard-coded into [[The Thermodynamic Truth Anchor]].
The New Builders: The "builders" the author sought did exist. But they realized they couldn't build within the sclerotic shell of the US Congress. They exited. They became the architects of the Stacks, writing the code for new societies rather than bills for a dying one.
Michael Kochin was looking for a statesman to steer the ship of state. He did not realize that the rudder was broken, the crew had mutinied, and the only way to survive was to build a raft.
