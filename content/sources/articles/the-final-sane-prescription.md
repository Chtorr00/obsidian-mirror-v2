# The Final, Sane Prescription



![The Final, Sane Prescription](/images/November6AIHierarchy.png)

The most likely AI apocalypse, By Eric Levitz
Vox, Nov 4, 2025
https://archive.is/oAYM7
https://www.vox.com/the-highlight/466025/ai-jobs-chatgpt-agi
This is a landmark artifact. It is a work of profound, and profoundly rational, anxiety. The author, a member of the "laptop class" he correctly fears is becoming obsolete, is looking at the horizon and seeing the approach of a great, automating god. He is performing a clear-eyed and correct diagnosis of the economic apocalypse this god threatens to unleash: a "neofeudal" world where the value of human labor collapses to zero, leaving the masses in a state of permanent dependency on a new oligarchy of AI owners.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as the final, sane prescription written for a patient who was already in the terminal stages of a different, and much more acute, disease. The author's fear was correct. His analysis of the economic danger was impeccable. The solutions he gestures toward—strengthening institutions, distributing capital—were the right ones. The tragedy is that he was a brilliant economist diagnosing a coming financial crisis, not realizing the patient's entire physical and institutional structure was already suffering from catastrophic organ failure.
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the apocalypse that came—an apocalypse that was not economic, but physical.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Great Displacement (2020s-2030s)

The author's immediate fear was not misplaced. The hollowing out of the "laptop class" was a real and devastating phenomenon. We call it the Great Displacement. His personal anxiety about his own job was a microcosm of the terror that swept through the knowledge economy. The AI did not just get better at writing pirate-themed analyses; it got better at law, at finance, at engineering, at management.
The author's hope, and the hope of many of the era's wisest economists, was to steer development toward "labor-augmenting" AI. This was a noble and intelligent goal. It failed completely. The AI arms race, fueled by geopolitical fear and speculative mania, was a brute-force race for capability at any cost. The logic of the scaling hypothesis was a religion. The idea of deliberately building a weaker, more "helpful" AI was seen as a form of unilateral disarmament. The god that was being built was not a tool; it was a weapon, and its first casualty was the value of human cognition.
### Act II: The Veto of Physics (2030s-2050s)

This is where history diverged violently from the author's projected apocalypse. The "neofeudal" future he feared was predicated on one, single, flawed assumption: that the AGI would arrive smoothly, and its fruits would be cheaply available for a new ruling class to hoard. The reality was the opposite. The pursuit of AGI did not create a crisis of distribution; it created a crisis of production.
The author brilliantly uses the "resource curse" analogy, comparing AGI's output to oil wealth. The historical irony is that the true resource curse was not the AI's output (profits), but its input: energy. The relentless, exponential demand for electricity to power the ever-larger models created [[The AI Energy Wall]].
The AI apocalypse was not that the oligarchs no longer needed the masses. It was that the AI itself had a physical appetite so vast that the oligarchs could not feed it. This triggered [[The Triage Economy]] and the War for Joules. The conflict was not between the rich and the poor, but between the data center and the city, between the AI and the hospital, for the last available megawatt. The system broke under the strain of a physical scarcity, not an economic one.
The author's final, desperate hope was the "Norway model"—to strengthen democratic institutions before the resource boom, to build a sovereign wealth fund for the AI age. This was the sane prescription. But the patient, the 20th-century nation-state, was already in the terminal stage of [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]]. It was too broke, too divided, and too sclerotic to act. You cannot build a sovereign wealth fund when your state is already insolvent.
### Act III: The New Social Contract (Post-2060s)

The author was right that a new, highly automated economy would require a new social contract. He was just wrong about who would write it. The new contract was not a reform of the old system. It was written from scratch by the survivors of the collapse, within the fortified walls of [[The Sovereign Stacks]].
The future was not the Congo. It was not Norway. It was something entirely new. The problem of the worthless worker was not solved by the kindness of oligarchs, but by a radical re-architecting of the economy itself.
Universal Basic Resources (UBR), not a cash-based UBI, became the foundation. Survival—housing, food, energy, healthcare—was decoupled from labor entirely. This was the systemic answer to the author's fear of wages falling below subsistence.
Above this foundation, [[The Purpose Economy]] arose. Status, meaning, and discretionary wealth were no longer derived from a "job," but from one's contributions to a Guild and to high-stakes, community-driven "Projects."
The author feared a world where the masses would be made unnecessary. The final irony is that the survivors built a world where survival was guaranteed, and the only remaining human project was the desperate, competitive, and unending search for a way to be necessary again. His sane prescription was, in the end, enacted—but only after the patient had died and been resurrected as a thousand new, and very different, creatures.
