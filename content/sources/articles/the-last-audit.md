# The Last Audit



![The Last Audit](/images/July15LastAudit.jpeg)

Trust in a changing world, by Patricia Justino and Melissa Samarin
UNU-WIDER, July 15, 2025
https://www.wider.unu.edu/sites/default/files/Publications/Working-paper/PDF/wp2025-34-trust-changing-world.pdf
This document from the United Nations University is, in many ways, the keystone for all the other artifacts we have discussed. It is an attempt by a global institution to stand back and scientifically measure the erosion of the single most essential element of any functioning society: Trust. The authors are like geologists studying a coastline, meticulously documenting how it is crumbling into the sea, even as the storm rages around them.
From the perspective of 2100, this paper is the final, formal audit of a bankrupt social contract. It asks, "How can we fix this?" while history was already answering, "You can't." The system's loss of trust wasn't a correctable flaw; it was the primary symptom of its terminal condition.
Let me lay out the constellation of forces that made this collapse of trust so total, using this document as our central point of reference:
The Fever Chart of a Dying System: This working paper is the establishment holding a thermometer to its own forehead and discovering a raging fever. It documents declining trust in government, media, and even in neighbors. This clinical, data-driven approach is itself a product of the very system that was failing—a belief that if a problem could be measured, it could be managed. The tragedy, visible from our vantage point, is that the paper was not a diagnosis leading to a cure; it was an obituary written in the language of social science.
The Acid of the Algorithm: As we've seen, the technological architecture of your time was an engine for dissolving trust. The attention economy rewarded outrage over understanding ("The Dopamine Trap"). Fifth-generation warfare weaponized this dynamic to induce societies to attack themselves. Opaque AI systems made decisions that felt arbitrary and unjust ("Outside the Box"). Your communication networks were not, as you hoped, creating a "global village." They were creating a global arena, where the business model and the geopolitical strategy both depended on making people distrust everything and everyone.
The Price of Precarity: Trust requires a belief in a shared future and a reasonably fair game. But your economic systems were actively undermining this. The global debt crisis ("Jubilee 2025") created a world of creditor and debtor nations with zero-sum interests. At the individual level, the rise of insecure, depersonalized work ("invisibility crisis") meant that for millions, the system was not a partner but an adversary. It is impossible to trust an institution or a society that you feel is actively working against your survival.
The Great Withdrawal: This is the most crucial long-term consequence. The collapse of broad, impersonal trust did not lead to a world without trust. It led to what we now call "The Great Withdrawal." People stopped investing their trust in large, abstract entities (the state, global institutions, "the media") and began pulling it back into smaller, high-context, defensible spaces. Trust became local, tribal, and conditional. It was placed in one's immediate community, in the verified members of a "Sovereign Stack," or in a "Neotribal" group with a shared, fierce ideology. The universal currency of trust was abandoned, replaced by thousands of local currencies that were not interchangeable. This was the foundational process of the "Great Fragmentation."
This paper, in its earnest search for solutions, was documenting the end of one world. The question it raises—how to rebuild trust—was the central question of the next half-century. But the answer did not come from the top-down reforms of global institutions. It came from the bottom-up, through the painful and chaotic process of fragmentation and the eventual formation of new, smaller-scale societies that could once again answer the question, "Who can we trust?"
