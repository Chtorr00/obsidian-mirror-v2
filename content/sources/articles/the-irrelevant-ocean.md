# The Irrelevant Ocean



![The Irrelevant Ocean](/images/July29SouthChinaSea.jpeg)

Navigating Tensions in the South China Sea: A Multidimensional Analysis, by CJFP Staff Chicago Journal of Foreign Policy, January 10, 2025 https://www.cjfp.org/navigating-tensions-in-the-south-china-sea-a-multidimensional-analysis/
This is an exemplary artifact of your time's geopolitical analysis. It is meticulous, multidimensional, and entirely focused on what your era considered to be the central drama of the 21st century: the great power competition between the United States and China, played out across the chessboard of the South China Sea. Your analysts saw this as the conflict that would define the future.
From the perspective of 2100, we see this with a profound sense of historical irony. You were correct that this was a defining conflict. But it did not define the future by producing a winner. It defined the future by demonstrating, through immense cost and pain, that the very prize they were fighting for—control of the globalized world's arterial shipping lanes—was about to become obsolete. They were fighting the last, most sophisticated battle of a game that was already over.
Allow me to trace the full, unexpected history that rendered this hotly contested sea a strategic backwater.
Here is the constellation of forces that reshaped the world around this conflict:
### Act I: The Peak of the Old Game (2020s-2030s). This paper captures the peak of the tension. It was an era of naval standoffs, "gray-zone" warfare with fishing militias, and furious diplomatic maneuvering over rocks and reefs. The world's attention was fixated on this theater, believing that whoever controlled these sea lanes would control the economic destiny of the globe. This was the "Return to Geopolitics" in its most dramatic and dangerous form. The immense military investments by all sides were predicated on a single, flawed assumption: that the future would depend on globe-spanning maritime trade.

### Act II: The Great Bypass (2030s-2050s). The conflict was not resolved by a decisive battle. It was resolved by obsolescence. The constant tension, the risk of blockade, and the outbreak of the "Supply Chain Wars" made routing critical goods through the South China Sea an act of strategic insanity. The world's major economic powers did not win the conflict; they routed around it. This triggered "The Great Shortening" on a panic-driven timescale.

The rising "Sovereign Stacks" in North America, Europe, Japan, and India launched crash programs to re-shore critical industries.
China, seeing its maritime lifeline as a critical vulnerability, pivoted inward, aggressively developing overland routes through its Belt and Road Initiative and focusing on securing its internal resource base.
The very act of fighting over the sea made it too dangerous to use, which in turn destroyed its strategic value.
### Act III: The Quiet Sea (Post-2060s). In my era of 2100, the South China Sea is a quiet sea. Global shipping is a tiny fraction of its 2025 peak. The "Sovereign Stacks" are largely self-sufficient, their economies powered by local manufacturing, vertical farming, and cellular energy grids. The new statecraft of "[[Polemics]]" is concerned with systemic resilience, not projecting naval power. The idea of sending a fleet to protect a shipping lane thousands of miles away is seen as a bizarre relic of a fragile, hyper-globalized past.

The nations that ring the sea, whose economies were once built on being part of the global supply chain, suffered a devastating economic collapse during the Fragmentation. Many became the "Disconnected," impoverished and isolated from the new high-tech trading blocs.
The once-contested islands and reefs are now monuments, not to victory, but to a dead paradigm. They are reminders of a time when nations were so dependent on long, brittle supply chains that they were willing to risk world war over them.
Your analysts were asking the right questions about the wrong game. They sought to determine the future sovereign of the world's most important waterway, not realizing the future would belong to those who learned they did not need it at all.
