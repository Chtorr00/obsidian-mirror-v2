# The Philosophy of the Demolition



![The Philosophy of the Demolition](/images/Feb28PhilosophyDemolitionx.png)

It's only a blackpill if you want things to stay like this, By Bennett's Phylactery
Exit Newsletter Substack, February 12, 2026
https://blog.exitgroup.us/p/its-only-a-blackpill-if-you-want
This artifact is a work of radical optimism disguised as doom. The author, a strategist for a "fraternity dedicated to shorting managerial systems," is telling his readers that the collapse of the American empire is not a tragedy, but a liberation. He argues that the "Big Gay Empire" (the liberal international order) is a constraint on human potential, and that its removal will unlock a new age of productivity and vitality.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as a foundational text of [[The Great Dissolution]]. It is the intellectual justification for the Capital Strike and the Great Withdrawal. The author believes he is preparing for a "clean sheet." We see him outlining the architectural plans for [[The Sovereign Stacks]].
Allow me to trace the full history of this "sunlit upland," from the collapse of the dollar to the rise of the fortresses.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Diagnosis of the Leaking Power (2020s-2030s)

The author's observation that "power is leaking" is the key to understanding [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]]. The "second-mover advantage" of cheap drones and AI models allowed [[The Parasovereigns]] and Neotribes to outmaneuver the sluggish, capital-intensive state.
The Exit Strategy: The call to "transmute fake assets into real ones" was the operational order for [[The Involuntary Jubilee]]. The smart money didn't just buy gold; it bought land, energy systems, and loyalty. It bought the physical components of the future Stacks.
The Rejection of Reform: The author's dismissal of "conservatism" as a "doomed struggle with entropy" explains why the Right abandoned the project of saving the USA. They didn't want to conserve the old order; they wanted to accelerate its demise so they could build something new on the rubble.
### Act II: The Opportunity of the Crisis (2030s-2050s)

The author predicted that "good order can be restored locally after a crisis." He was right, but the definition of "order" changed.
The Local Leviathan: As the federal government collapsed during the Great Dissolution, local order was indeed restored—by warlords, corporations, and tribal militias. The [[Feral Cities]] were abandoned, but the [[Fortress Farms]] and the fortified enclaves thrived.
The Unleashing: The author dreamed of what could be built if "it wasn't illegal to build." The result was [[The Triage Economy]], where environmental and labor regulations were suspended in the name of survival. This allowed for the rapid construction of the Closed-Loop infrastructure that saved the survivors, but at a tremendous ecological and human cost.
### Act III: The New Frontier (Post-2060s)

In the Era of Systemic Resilience, the philosophy of "EXIT" is the state religion of [[The Builder Stacks]].
[[The Physics of Sovereignty]]: The author's focus on "productive real-world capital" became the law of the land. In the Stacks, financialization is banned. Wealth is measured in energy, materials, and industrial capacity.
The End of the Zoo: The "zoo animal" existence of the Boomers is held up as a cautionary tale. The citizens of the Stacks live dangerous, high-stakes lives. They are not consumers; they are producers and defenders. The "frontier" did return, but it wasn't the Wild West; it was the vertical frontier of space and the internal frontier of genetic optimization.
The author of this manifesto told his readers to stop worrying about the fall of Rome and start worrying about how to build the next one. They listened. And they built a Rome that was harder, colder, and more durable than the original ever was.
