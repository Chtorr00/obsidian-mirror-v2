# The Schism of the Stroller



![The Schism of the Stroller](/images/March6SchismStroller.png)

A parents’ group tried to establish boundaries for discussion on their WhatsApp chat. It led to a schism.

This artifact is a dispatch from the domestic front of [[The Great Dissolution]]. The author, a journalist and mother, is chronicling the breakup of a neighborhood parenting group. She sees a story about "boundaries" and "political polarization." She frames it as a local conflict exacerbated by national tensions.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as a primary source document for [[The Great Physical Sorting]]. The "schism" described here was not an argument; it was the cellular division of the American body politic. The parents of Petworth were not just disagreeing; they were sorting themselves into the embryonic forms of two distinct and incompatible civilizations.
Allow me to trace the full, divergent history of these two groups, from the WhatsApp chats of your time to the separate realities of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Incompatibility of the Commons (2020s-2030s)

The conflict in the chat reveals the central truth of [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]]: there was no longer a "neutral" space.
The Politicization of Everything: The "Free Range" parents correctly understood that in a collapsing empire, parenting is political. Every choice—schooling, safety, values—is a survival strategy. Their demand for the group to "take a stand" was a recognition that the Crisis of Meaning had become total.
The Retreat to Comfort: The "Peanuts" board, prioritizing "neutrality" and "security clearances," represented the Administrative Stack (Blue) mindset. They wanted to maintain the illusion of normalcy within a fortified bubble. Their refusal to engage with the chaos outside (ICE raids, protests) was the psychological precursor to the Gilded Cage of the coastal elites.
### Act II: The Formalization of the Tribe (2030s-2050s)

The split did not heal. It hardened. The two groups didn't just have different chats; they built different worlds.
The Activist Enclave: The "Parenting in Community" group evolved into a Neotribe. As [[The Great Fragmentation]] accelerated, their "mutual aid" networks became essential survival infrastructure in the [[Feral Cities]]. They rejected the state and built a parallel society based on "solidarity" and "resistance." They were the ancestors of the [[Sanctuary Stacks]].
The Loyalist Enclave: The "Petworth Peanuts" remnants, with their federal jobs and security clearances, became the core population of the Washington Green Zone. They clung to the federal apparatus until the bitter end, maintaining a "civil" facade while the city around them burned. Their "neutrality" became a form of collaboration with whatever regime held the White House, until [[The Involuntary Jubilee]] finally dissolved their paychecks.
### Act III: The Divergent Curricula (Post-2060s)

In the Era of Systemic Resilience, the children of these two groups are unrecognizable to each other.
The Sanctuary Child: Raised in the ethos of "Parenting in Community," the children of the Sanctuary Stacks are raised to question authority, value the collective, and resist the "paramilitary Gestapo." Their education is ideological and communal.
The Administrative Child: The descendants of the Peanuts group are the functionaries of [[The New Priesthood]]. They are raised in [[The New Academies]] to value order, protocol, and the stability of the Stack above all else. They view "political organizing" as a dangerous vector for [[The Great Contamination]].
The author wondered if parenting was "always political." We have the hindsight to know that parenting is the ultimate political act, because it is the manufacturing of the next generation's loyalties. The schism in the chat was the first battle of a war that would be fought by the children playing with battery-operated candles.
The author of this piece saw a neighborhood fight. We see the tectonic plates of a civilization pulling apart, taking the families standing on the fault line with them.
