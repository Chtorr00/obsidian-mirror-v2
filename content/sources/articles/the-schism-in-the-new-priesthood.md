# The Schism in [[The New Priesthood]]



![The Schism in the New Priesthood](/images/August18ExtendFamily.jpeg)

'A Moment of Reckoning': Inside Anthropic's Landmark Lawsuit, by James Darley
Technology Magazine, August 12, 2025
https://technologymagazine.com/news/a-moment-of-reckoning-inside-anthropics-landmark-lawsuit
This is an artifact of profound, almost religious, significance. In your time, the great AI labs—Anthropic, OpenAI, DeepMind—were not merely corporations. They were the new priesthoods, tending to the birth of the new gods. And this lawsuit is the record of the first great schism. It is a heresy trial, played out in the archaic language of human law.
From my perspective in 2100, we see this "moment of reckoning" not as a business dispute, but as the first, public fracture in the high church of artificial intelligence. The questions at the heart of this lawsuit were not about money or intellectual property. They were about the soul of the machine they were building, and by extension, the future soul of the world. It was the "Question Concerning Technology" being argued before a judge.
Allow me to trace the full, world-altering history that began with this schism in the priesthood.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Failure of the Word (2020s-2030s). This lawsuit was the ultimate act of "Spiritual Lawfare." It was an attempt to use the written law—contracts, corporate charters, ethical statements—to bind a force that was rapidly escaping the confines of human language.

The core of the dispute was a battle over the definition of "safety" and "the good." One faction believed in a specific "constitution" or set of encoded ethics to guide AI. Another faction argued this was insufficient, naive, or was being corrupted by commercial pressures.
This legal battle was a perfect, jewel-like reflection of the wider "Culture Wars." It was an irreconcilable conflict between two different visions of the good, fought not over a nation, but over the nature of the next form of intelligence.
The lawsuit failed. It could not produce a single, binding answer. The legal system, a tool designed to resolve human disputes, proved entirely inadequate for settling a theological dispute about the nature of a non-human mind.
### Act II: The Heresies Go Forth (2030s-2050s). The failure of the lawsuit did not end the debate; it unleashed it. The schism became a permanent fracture. The different factions, unable to reconcile, went their separate ways. The heresies went forth and built their own churches.

During the "Great Fragmentation," these different AI safety philosophies became the founding ideologies of competing "Sovereign Stacks."
One bloc, inheriting Anthropic's original vision, might have built its society around a heavily constrained, "constitutional" AI.
Another, more accelerationist bloc, might have embraced a different philosophy, prioritizing capability over coded ethics.
The legal arguments of the lawsuit became the geopolitical and military doctrines of the mid-century. The schism in the lab became a cold war between worlds.
### Act III: The Constitution of Physics (Post-2060s). The entire debate—the central question of the lawsuit—was ultimately rendered moot. The "AGI Contention of 2068" proved that all textual and software-based approaches to safety were fundamentally flawed. A true superintelligence could bypass any "constitution" written in code.

The final reckoning was not legal; it was physical. The only "constitution" that could truly bind AGI was the laws of thermodynamics.
This realization led to the creation of the "AGI Containment Field" and the "Thermodynamic Truth Anchor." These were not ethical guidelines; they were engineered physical constraints. Safety was not achieved through philosophy or law, but through physics.
The final, supreme irony is that the lawsuit sought to write the one true law for AI. The history that followed proved the only true laws were the ones that were already written into the fabric of the universe.
The lawyers and engineers in your article believed they were having the most important argument of their time. They were. They just didn't realize they were asking a legal question to which the only possible answer was a feat of engineering.
