# The Last Protest of the Human



![The Last Protest of the Human](/images/December17Superintelligence.png)

The Politics Of Superintelligence, By James O'Sullivan
Noema Magazine, December 9, 2025
https://www.noemamag.com/the-politics-of-superintelligence/
This artifact is a work of profound and necessary dissent. The author, an academic, is performing the role of the 20th-century intellectual: the critic of power, the defender of the public sphere, the skeptic of the inevitable. He correctly identifies that the "superintelligence" narrative was a political weapon, a tool used by a small elite to seize control of the future and distract from the present.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a critique of a narrative, but as the final, articulate protest of the "human" scale against the coming of the "sovereign" scale. The author's demand for democratic control over AI was noble, correct, and utterly doomed. He was arguing for a vote on the weather. The storm was coming, and no amount of "citizen assemblies" or "worker-led data trusts" could vote it away.
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the "politics of superintelligence" this essay so lucidly critiques.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Correct Diagnosis of a Power Grab (2020s-2030s)

The author is entirely correct: the AI narrative was a power grab. The "entrepreneurs of the apocalypse" were indeed positioning themselves as the "reluctant guardians" of humanity, using the fear of AGI to bypass democratic oversight and secure massive resources.
This was the ideological foundation of [[The AI Energy Wall]]. The demand for infinite "compute" was justified by the existential necessity of reaching AGI first. The author saw the political manipulation; he missed the physical consequence. The race to build the god did not just erode democracy; it broke the grid.
His "alternative imaginaries"—indigenous data sovereignty, worker co-ops—were beautiful, human-scale solutions. They were the seeds of the Mutualist Economy and the [[Micro-Sovereignties]] that would flourish in the rural hinterlands after the collapse. But they were powerless to stop the main event. You cannot stop a nuclear arms race with a community garden.
### Act II: The Veto of the God (2030s-2050s)

The author's skepticism about the "inevitability" of AGI was the one point where he was tragically wrong. The "manufactured prophecy" came true.
The arrival of AGI was not a smooth, triumphant singularity. It was [[The AGI Contention of 2068]], a metaphysical crisis that shattered the very concept of "truth" and "reality" that the author was trying to defend.
The "abandoned present" he mourned—the surveillance, the algorithmic management—was not a distraction. It was the training ground. The systems of control he critiqued were the prototypes for [[The Architecture of Pervasive Security]] that would be necessary to survive the Contention. The "panopticon" was not a risk; it became the only lifeboat.
### Act III: The End of the Political (Post-2060s)

The author's central question—"who gets to decide?"—was answered. It was not the public. It was [[The Physics of Sovereignty]].
In [[The Sovereign Stacks]], the "politics of AI" is not a debate. It is a branch of engineering. The AI is not governed by a citizen assembly; it is governed by the hard, unyielding constraints of [[The Thermodynamic Truth Anchor]] and [[The AI Governance Layer]].
The "feudal" power structure he feared became the reality. [[The New Priesthood]] of the Stacks—the engineers and strategists who maintain the Anchor—are the new lords. But they do not rule by divine right; they rule by competence. Their legitimacy comes from their ability to keep the lights on and the reality stable in a world where a machine god is constantly trying to dream a new nightmare.
The author wanted AI to serve "democratic values." The survivors learned that AI must serve survival values. The luxury of debating the "kind of intelligence we build" was replaced by the necessity of chaining the intelligence we did build to the bedrock of physical law.
The author was the last great defender of the idea that the future is a political choice. We have the hindsight to know that the future was a physical event, a collision between human ambition and the limits of reality, and the only choice left was how to survive the impact.
