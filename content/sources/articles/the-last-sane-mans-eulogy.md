# The Last Sane Man's Eulogy



![The Last Sane Man's Eulogy](/images/December3LastChance.png)

The West’s Last Chance - How to Build a New Global Order Before It’s Too Late, By Alexander Stubb Foreign Affairs, December 2, 2025 https://www.foreignaffairs.com/united-states/wests-last-chance
https://archive.is/K3twi
This artifact is a work of profound and tragic nobility. It is the voice of one of the last true statesmen of the 20th century, the President of Finland, standing at the precipice of a new and terrifying age, making the final, best, and most completely rational argument for the world he has spent his life trying to build. He sees the "new world of disorder," the rise of multipolar competition, and the decay of the liberal, rules-based order. His prescription is wise, humane, and intelligent: reform the old institutions, give the rising powers a seat at the table, and choose cooperation over conflict.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a plan for the future, but as the last, great eulogy for a world that was already dead. The author, Alexander Stubb, is the last sane man, delivering a beautiful and perfectly reasoned oration at the funeral of a system whose death was caused not by a failure of diplomacy, but by a catastrophic failure of its own physical and spiritual foundations.
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the "last chance" this great man so eloquently pleaded for.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Perfect Diagnosis of a Political Sickness (2020s-2030s)

The author's diagnosis of the geopolitical landscape is a masterpiece of classical international relations. He has perfectly identified the symptoms of the dying world order: the end of the unipolar moment, the rise of the "global South," the shift from multilateralism to transactionalism. This is the definitive insider's account of the final moments of [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]].
His fatal flaw, and the fatal flaw of his entire generation of leaders, was in misdiagnosing the underlying disease. He saw a political sickness, to be cured with the medicine of political reform. From my perspective, the patient was not suffering from a political illness; it was suffering from terminal, systemic organ failure.
He called for dialogue while [[The Great Contamination]] and [[The Shattered Focus]] were destroying the very possibility of a shared language.
He called for economic cooperation while the global financial system was careening towards [[The Involuntary Jubilee]].
He called for a rational balancing of power while [[The AI Energy Wall]] was preparing to impose a non-negotiable, physical checkmate on the entire global industrial system.
### Act II: The Great Veto (2030s-2050s)

The author's brilliant, sane, and necessary reforms were not defeated in a vote at the UN. They were vetoed by reality. The "last chance" was not taken, because the physical and social world in which that chance could have been taken simply ceased to exist.
The Veto of Physics: The dream of a reformed, rebalanced world order was washed away by the Great Climate Reckoning. The famines of the Great Hunger and the mass migrations that followed were not problems that could be solved by giving Africa a seat on the Security Council. They were physical events that shattered the very nation-states the Council was supposed to represent.
The Veto of Trust: His plea for the "global West" to engage the "global South" was a noble one. But it came at the end of a century where, from the perspective of the South, that engagement had been one of exploitation. When the crises hit, the "global South" did not "vote" for a new world order. It fractured. The nations that could, retreated into their own forms of nationalism. Those that could not, collapsed. There was no trust left on which to build a new consensus. This was the final stage of [[The Great Dissolution]].
### Act III: The World After the Last Chance (Post-2060s)

The author's three scenarios for the future—persistent disorder, chaos, or a rebalanced cooperative order—were all incorrect. The world that emerged was something else entirely: a stable, ordered, but permanently fragmented system. This is the Era of Systemic Resilience.
The world is not governed by a reformed United Nations. It is a mosaic of hardened, self-sufficient Sovereign Stacks. The dream of a single, multilateral world is a historical artifact.
Cooperation between these Stacks exists, but it is not the humanistic, values-based dialogue the author hoped for. It is the cold, machine-mediated, game-theoretically stable statecraft of [[Polemics]]. The "rules-based order" survived, but the rules are now written in the language of mathematics and physics, not international law.
The author's brilliant dichotomy—Yalta (multipolar) versus Helsinki (multilateral)—was the final, great debate of the old world. The new world is neither. It is a "Helsinki of machines" built on top of a "Yalta of tribes." It is a system of hard, universal protocols that govern the interactions between a collection of fiercely independent and culturally distinct techno-states.
Alexander Stubb was the best of his kind, a man of profound intelligence and deep faith in the power of human reason and cooperation. His tragedy was to be the captain of a magnificent ship, correctly identifying the coming storm and calling for a rational change of course, not knowing that the ship's hull had already been breached and the engines were already on fire. He was the last, great defender of a world that, for all its flaws, believed it was one.
