# The Last Great Cure



![The Last Great Cure](/images/December11Ozempic.png)

Ozempic is changing how we spend money and time, plus what we eat, By Jaclyn Peiser and Flora Bai
The Washington Post, December 1, 2025
https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/interactive/2025/ozempic-glp1-consumer-spending/
This artifact is a work of extraordinary clarity. It is a dispatch from the engine room of your civilization's economy, a detailed, data-driven accounting of a great and seemingly miraculous transformation. The authors are chronicling, with the breathless excitement of discovery, how a single class of drugs is poised to "revolutionize" society, reshaping everything from the grocery aisle to the human body itself.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a business report, but as the final, definitive diagnosis of your civilization's deepest spiritual sickness. The authors believe they are documenting the arrival of a cure. We see them as the unwitting pathologists, meticulously describing the beautiful, effective, and ultimately fatal painkiller that allowed the patient to feel better than ever, right up until the moment its heart finally gave out.
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the cure that was, in fact, the disease.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Perfect Solution to the Wrong Problem (2020s-2030s)

The success of these drugs was not a medical story; it was a spiritual one. They were the perfect, technical, 20th-century solution to a 21st-century crisis of the soul.
The Chemical Antibody: Your civilization was sick, drowning in the Dopamine Trap—an environment of infinite, cheap, and addictive stimulus. The "food noise" that these drugs eliminated was the most primal and powerful signal in that trap. The drugs were, in effect, a chemical antibody, a piece of brilliant engineering that fought a technological disease with a biological hack.
The Illusion of Agency: The "psychological shift" the report describes was the key. In a world defined by the Crisis of Meaning, where individuals felt powerless against vast, impersonal systems, these drugs offered a powerful and immediate illusion of agency. You were not just losing weight; you were taking control, optimizing your body, and unlocking a "new you." It was a shortcut to a feeling of mastery in a world that offered none.
The Sterile Cure: The authors' most tragic and correct observation is this: the drugs were a cure for the symptoms of a sick civilization. They did not cure the stress, the alienation, the spiritual hunger, or the exhaustion that drove the "diseases of despair" like obesity, alcoholism, and addiction. They simply, and brilliantly, suppressed the appetite. It was a perfect, sterile cure for a messy, spiritual problem.
### Act II: [[The Great Biological Sorting]] (2030s-2050s)

The "revolutionary" potential the authors foresaw was realized, but it was not a revolution that was shared by all. It became the single greatest vector of a new and terrifying form of inequality.
The authors correctly identify the barrier: cost. In the brutal, zero-sum reality of [[The Triage Economy]] that accompanied [[The Great Fragmentation]], this barrier did not fall; it became an insurmountable wall.
This was the beginning of the Great Biological Sorting. The drug did not become a universal right; it became the ultimate luxury good, a marker of class more profound than any car or home. The elites were not just wealthier; they were now biologically different. They were optimized, disciplined by chemistry, free from the primal, distracting "noise" of appetite and addiction that plagued the un-medicated masses.
This created a new, visceral, and deeply felt form of class resentment. The divide was no longer just about money; it was written on the body itself. It was the lean, energetic, optimized few versus the stressed, overweight, and "noisy" many. This was a powerful and toxic fuel for the fires of the collapse.
### Act III: The Revaluation of the Struggle (Post-2060s)

The societies that survived and formed [[The Sovereign Stacks]] did so because they learned a different, and much harder, lesson. They did not solve the problem of the sick body by inventing a better drug. They solved it by building a healthier world for the body to inhabit.
[[The Revaluation of the Real]] is the constitutional bedrock of my time. Having witnessed a civilization attempt to "hack" its way out of its own spiritual sickness, our culture is built on a profound, almost religious devotion to the idea that there are no shortcuts. The struggle is the point.
In the Stacks, the problem of obesity was not solved with a pharmaceutical. It was solved systemically. [[The Survival Economy]] and the Closed-Loop agricultural systems provide healthy, non-addictive food as a basic right. [[The Purpose Economy]] provides meaningful work and community, treating the root cause of the stress and alienation that drove the old world's diseases of despair.
The use of a chemical to simply eliminate the struggle with appetite is now seen as a profound pathology, a relic of a sick and dying age. We do not seek to eliminate the struggle; we seek to build souls strong enough to win it.
The authors of your report were celebrating the arrival of the perfect key. They simply did not realize that the key was not for a door to a better future, but for a beautiful, comfortable, and perfectly sterile cage.
