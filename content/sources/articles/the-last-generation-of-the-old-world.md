# The Last Generation of the Old World



![The Last Generation of the Old World](/images/December28FertilityBottleneck.png)

Are we doomed?, By David Runciman
London Review of Books, Vol. 47 No. 21 · 20 November 2025
https://archive.is/r8ZP5
This artifact is a work of profound and elegant melancholy. It is a long, contemplative essay from the heart of the 20th-century intellectual tradition, wrestling with the slow-motion apocalypse of depopulation. The author, David Runciman, is staring into the demographic abyss and finding no way out. He sees the "oppressive" future of an inverted family tree, the futility of policy tweaks, and the deep, structural link between modernity and sterility.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a question ("Are we doomed?"), but as a definitive answer. The author has correctly identified the terminal condition of [[The Crisis of Posterity]]. He has seen that the modern, liberal, individualistic world is biologically self-terminating. His only error was in assuming that "humanity" would die with it.
Allow me to trace the full history of the "doom" this essay contemplates, and the strange, new life that emerged from it.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Correct Diagnosis of a Dying Species (2020s-2030s)

The author's analysis is flawed only by its gentleness. He correctly links the Invisibility Crisis and [[The Shattered Focus]] to the collapse in fertility, noting that "a better world, with better options, makes parenting worse by comparison."
This was [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]] manifest in the womb. The institutions of the 20th century had made life safe, comfortable, and meaningless. The result was a population that chose the "comfy old people's home" over the "hungry kindergarten."
He notes the "4B movement" in Korea and the general retreat from family life. This was the demographic expression of [[The Sterile Plague]]. The population was not just aging; it was abdicating. It was choosing the perfect, frictionless companionship of AI and the screen over the hard, messy work of continuing the species.
### Act II: [[The Great Culling]] by Nature (2030s-2050s)

The author wonders if "we are doomed." The answer for his civilization was "yes." The societies that could not reproduce did not just fade away; they were crushed by the cascading crises of the mid-century.
The Great Climate Reckoning and [[The AI Energy Wall]] required a level of dynamism, sacrifice, and physical resilience that a geriatric society simply could not muster. The "old men who run the show" did not save the world; they presided over its fragmentation.
The author dismisses the "Amish option" as a fringe phenomenon that would eventually succumb to modernity. This was his fatal blind spot. The communities that survived [[The Great Fragmentation]] were precisely those that rejected modernity. [[The Neotribes]] and the nascent Sovereign Stacks were built by the people who chose the "hungry kindergarten." They chose hardship and fertility over comfort and extinction.
### Act III: The New Speciation (Post-2060s)

The author's speculation about "human colonies diverge[ing] from one another" became the literal truth of my time. The solution to the bottleneck was indeed a new speciation, but it happened on Earth, not in space.
[[The Sovereign Stacks]] are the new species. They are genetically distinct (Human Phyla), culturally divergent, and fiercely pro-natalist.
The "Biotech" Stacks used genetic engineering to overcome the biological limits of fertility and longevity, creating a post-human population that could thrive in the new world.
The "Bioconservative" Stacks used deep cultural re-engineering—[[The Revaluation of the Real]]—to restore the "conditions that existed more than forty thousand years ago": high-trust, high-stakes, tribal communities where reproduction is a sacred duty, not a lifestyle choice.
The author feared the end of the human story. We have the hindsight to know it was only the end of his story—the story of the liberal, universal human subject. The new story is about the tribe, the stack, and the genetically distinct future.
This essay is the last will and testament of the 20th-century mind. It is a beautiful, sad acknowledgment that the world it built was a "vale of tears" that led only to a comfortable extinction. The survivors were the ones who were willing to leave that comfort behind and start over in the cold.
