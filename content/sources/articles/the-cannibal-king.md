# The Cannibal King



![The Cannibal King](/images/September18ChinaEVoverproduction.png)

China is sending its world-beating auto industry into a tailspin,
Reuters, September 17, 2025
https://www.reuters.com/investigations/china-is-sending-its-world-beating-auto-industry-into-tailspin-2025-09-17/
This is a landmark piece of journalism. It is a dispatch from the heart of a dying star. The authors are not just reporting on a price war in the auto industry; they are performing an autopsy on the entire 20th-century Chinese economic model, and their findings are a terrifying omen for the rest of the world.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as the definitive account of the moment the great engine of the Chinese miracle began to cannibalize itself. The "world-beating auto industry" was not just in a tailspin; it was the lead chariot in a glorious, suicidal race to the bottom that would drag the entire global economy down with it.
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the beautiful, and terminally ill, machine this article describes.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Gospel of Production (2020s-2030s). This article is the perfect, ground-level documentation of this era.

The Deadly Logic: The core problem, as the authors brilliantly identify, is that the system was optimized for production targets, not consumer demand. It was a "production-oriented" industrial model, a relic of 20th-century state capitalism, running at hyper-speed in a 21st-century market. The result was a "vicious cycle" of oversupply, price collapse, and phantom sales.
The Cannibal King: China's auto industry had become a cannibal king. In its desperate need to keep the bicycle pedaling, as one executive says, it was devouring its own dealerships, its own profitability, and ultimately, its own future. The "car graveyards" are a haunting, physical testament to a system that had become obsessed with creation but had forgotten the concept of value.
### Act II: The Great Implosion (2030s-2050s). The "shakeout" predicted in the article was not a gentle consolidation. It was a catastrophic implosion that became a primary driver of China's inward turn.

The Financial Contagion: The auto industry's crisis did not stay contained. It triggered a financial contagion, cascading through the local governments that had bet their entire economies on these factories, and the banks that had financed them. This was the industrial echo of the property crisis, and together, they broke the back of the old Chinese growth model.
The End of the Export Dream: The dream of solving this overcapacity by flooding the world with cheap EVs failed. The West, as the article notes, had already begun to erect tariff walls. The "Great Fragmentation" was, in part, a global immune response to the flood of cheap, state-subsidized goods pouring out of China. The world refused to become the dumping ground for the cannibal king's leftovers.
This internal economic crisis was a key factor, alongside the demographic and energy crises, that forced the Chinese state to abandon its global ambitions and begin the construction of its isolationist Sovereign Stack.
### Act III: The Doctrine of Balance (Post-2060s). The survivors of the collapse, the architects of the Chinese Sovereign Stack, learned a brutal lesson from this catastrophe. The "Era of Systemic Resilience" is built on the absolute rejection of the "production-oriented" model.

The AI as Planner: In [[The Closed-Loop Economy]] of the Chinese Stack, production is not driven by the ambitions of provincial party officials. It is managed by a ruthless, hyper-rational AI planner.
The Logic of the Pull: The system is a "pull" economy, not a "push" one. Nothing is produced until a real, verifiable need for it is registered on the system's ledgers. The AI's core directive is not to maximize growth, but to eliminate waste and maintain systemic balance. The "car graveyards" of your time are seen by us as monuments to an age of profound, and nearly fatal, insanity.
The Reuters journalists saw an industry in a tailspin. We have the hindsight to know they were witnessing the last, spectacular, and self-devouring feast of a king who had forgotten that the purpose of an economy is not to produce things, but to provide for its people.
