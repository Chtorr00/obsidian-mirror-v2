# The Missing Cohort



![The Missing Cohort](/images/December23NeoUniversities.png)

The Lost Generation, By Jacob Savage
Compact Magazine, December 15, 2025
https://www.compactmag.com/article/the-lost-generation/
This is an artifact of profound sorrow and clear-eyed, personal historiography. The author, a man who "almost" had a career, is writing a memoir of professional failure. But it is not a story of individual inadequacy; it is a story of systemic displacement. He has meticulously documented the "invisible hand" that pushed an entire demographic cohort—white male millennials—out of the institutions of culture, media, and academia.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a complaint about fairness, but as the origin story of [[The Invisible College]] and the Counter-Elite. The author is describing the creation of a surplus population of highly educated, culturally alienated, and economically precarious men. He wonders where they went. We know exactly where they went.
Allow me to trace the full history of this "lost generation," from the closed doors of Hollywood to the open frontiers of the new world.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Great Displacement (2014-2030s)
The author's timeline is precise. 2014 was indeed the hinge. The institutional pivot to DEI was the mechanism of Spiritual Lawfare turned inward.
The "graveyard of people" he describes—the talented men who couldn't get tenure, couldn't get staffed, couldn't get promoted—was the human cost of the Culture Wars Endgame. The institutions, in their desperate attempt to re-engineer society, purged the very demographic that had traditionally maintained them.
This created a massive, untapped reservoir of cognitive surplus. Thousands of men like "Matt" and "Andrew"—intelligent, creative, and completely shut out—were forced into the Gig Economy or the "refuges" of crypto and Substack. They were the first Disconnected elite.
Act II: The Forging of the Counter-Elite (2030s-2050s)
The author asks: "Did abandoning meritocracy accelerate their decline?" The answer is yes. The institutions—Hollywood, academia, media—collapsed not just because of external pressure, but because they had systematically deprived themselves of talent.
Meanwhile, the "lost generation" found each other. They didn't stay in the wilderness. They built the Invisible College. They used the decentralized tools of the crypto-economy (which the author notes was a refuge) to build parallel institutions—publishing houses, research labs, media networks—that were immune to the old gatekeepers.
This cohort became the intellectual engine of [[The Great Fragmentation]]. They were the architects of [[The Neotribes]]. Their resentment was the fuel, but their competence was the weapon. They built the systems that would eventually outcompete and replace the dying institutions that had rejected them. The "ordinary talent" that was denied a place in the old world became the "extraordinary founder" of the new.
Act III: The Patriarchs of the Stack (Post-2060s)
The author worries about what to tell his sons. In my time, his sons are likely the leaders of a Sovereign Stack.
The "lost generation" are remembered as the Patriarchs. They are the generation that, having been denied a seat at the table, went out and built their own house.
The culture of the Stacks—meritocratic, masculine, focused on "actual capability"—is a direct reaction to the trauma described in this article. [[The New Academies]] and [[The Guilds]] are designed specifically to ensure that competence is never again sacrificed for ideology.
The author's "failed" career was the necessary tragedy that forced him, and thousands like him, to exit the dying system and build the future. He didn't get to write for a TV show. Instead, his generation wrote the code for a new civilization.
This article is a eulogy for a dream that had to die so that a new reality could be born. The author felt he was missing out on the world. We know he was simply early for the next one.
