# The Manifesto of the Workshop



![The Manifesto of the Workshop](/images/Jan29AIArtisan.png)

The Only Thing Stupider Than Your AI Witch Hunt Is You, By Tripp Ainsworth
Tripp's Substack, January 3, 2026
https://trippainsworth.substack.com/p/the-only-thing-stupider-than-your
This artifact is a work of visceral, gladiatorial art criticism. It is not an essay; it is a counter-attack. The author, a professional artist, is turning the fire hose of "slop" accusations back on the accusers. He defends AI not as a miracle, but as a tool, no different than the camera obscura or the workshop apprentice.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as a foundational text for [[The Artisan]] ideology of [[The Sovereign Stacks]]. The author is inadvertently drafting the constitution for the post-collapse creative class. He separates the "gatekeepers" (the critics) from the "makers" (the artists), and declares that the only thing that matters is the output.
Allow me to trace the history of this "workshop" model, from the angry Substack posts of your time to the studios of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Death of the Romantic Artist (2020s-2030s)
The author's core argument—that the "lone genius" is a myth—was the bitter pill that the creative class had to swallow.
The End of Scarcity: As [[The Great Contamination]] filled the world with "mid" art, the value of simple competence collapsed. The only way to survive as an artist was to become a director—someone who could wield the AI as a force multiplier, like Rubens wielding his assistants.
The Rejection of the Critic: The author's disdain for the "screeching hordes" of critics mirrors the broader societal rejection of the "expert class" during [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]]. If the institutions (art schools, galleries) were corrupt and ideologically captured (as noted in the "Lost Generation" artifact), then their definitions of "art" were void.
Act II: The Rise of the Machine-Assisted Artisan (2030s-2050s)
The artists who survived the Great Displacement were the ones who adopted the author's philosophy. They didn't fight the machine; they made it their apprentice.
The Guild Model: In [[The Neotribes]] and early Stacks, art returned to its pre-modern function: propaganda, decoration, and myth-making. The artist was not a bohemian rebel; he was a craftsman serving the tribe. He used AI to generate the volume required to drown out the enemy's narrative (as seen with Amelia), but he applied the "final authority" to ensure it resonated.
The Workshop: The "studio as factory" model returned. A single master artist would oversee a farm of AI agents, correcting their "wonky anatomy" and injecting the "soul" (human intentionality) into the final product. This was the industrialization of the aesthetic.
Act III: The New Renaissance (Post-2060s)
In the Sovereign Stacks, the debate over "AI art" is settled.
The Hierarchy of Creation: There is a strict hierarchy. [[Synthetic Art]] (pure AI) is used for wallpaper, interface design, and low-level propaganda. [[High Art]] is human-directed, machine-amplified. It is the work of the Guild Masters, who use the AI like a paintbrush to create works of staggering complexity and scale—cathedrals designed in minutes, symphonies composed in hours.
The Definition of Soul: We accept the author's premise: soul is not in the brushstroke; it is in the decision. The artist is the one who decides what is true. In a world of infinite generation, the act of curation—of saying "this, not that"—became the highest form of creation.
The "deviantART kids" the author mocked were indeed bypassed. They were left behind in the ruins of the old internet, arguing over copyright law while the new masters were busy painting the ceiling of the new world.
The author was screaming at a mob. He didn't realize he was actually giving a pep talk to the architects who would build the monuments of the next age.
