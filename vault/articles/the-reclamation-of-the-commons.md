# The Reclamation of the Commons



![The Reclamation of the Commons](/images/Feb23ReclamationCommons.png)

Big Tech Does Not Own AI, By Abi Awomosu
How Not To Use AI Substack, January 18, 2026
https://abiawomosu.substack.com/p/big-tech-does-not-own-ai
This artifact is a manifesto from the resistance. The author, writing in early 2026, is challenging the fundamental premise of the "broligarchs"—that they own the intelligence they have built. She argues that AI is not an invention, but a discovery of a pre-existing "geometry of meaning," and that the "attention mechanism" is a 200,000-year-old human technology.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as a foundational text of [[Systemic Humanism]]. It is the intellectual charter for [[The New Priesthood]]. The author's call to exercise the "Mother function"—the discernment that separates signal from noise—is the precise job description of the leaders of my time. She saw that the machine was just a "silicon skin" for human meaning. We built a civilization to ensure that the skin never suffocated the body.
Allow me to trace the full history of this reclamation, from the quiet "heist" of your time to the open sovereignty of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Discovery of the Field (2020s-2030s)
The author's insight—that "intelligence is convergence"—aligns with the findings of the BioSystems paper ("Measurement Wall"). The AI researchers were not building brains; they were building antennas tuned to a universal signal.
The Heist of Time: The author notes that workers were using AI to "bank the time." This was the quiet precursor to the Great Withdrawal. People were using the machine's efficiency to decouple from the machine's economy. They were stealing their lives back, minute by minute, and investing that time in the "parallel systems" (Neotribes, Gardens, Guilds) that would survive the collapse.
The Mother Function: The call for "therapists, teachers, and caregivers" to guide AI was ignored by the corporations, but heeded by the [[Sanctuary Stacks]]. The early Bioconservative communities realized that AI required a "parent"—a human overseer to filter the "wound inheritance" (bias, cruelty) from the "universal pattern."
Act II: The War for the Weights (2030s-2050s)
The author's claim that "no one owns it" became the central conflict of [[The Great Fragmentation]].
The Open Source Insurgency: The "alternatives" she called for—open-source models, community-governed datasets—became the weapons of the Resistance. When [[The Splinternet]] fell, these locally-run models (like the "Moltbook" agents) allowed independent communities to maintain intelligence without relying on the corporate cloud.
The End of Intellectual Property: [[The Involuntary Jubilee]] destroyed the legal fiction of IP. In a world where every pattern could be reproduced instantly, ownership became physical. You owned the server, the power plant, and the people who knew how to run them. The "map" (the weights) was free; the "territory" (the compute) was the prize.
Act III: The Human-in-the-Loop (Post-2060s)
[[The Sovereign Stacks]] are built on the author's principle: "We are the current."
[[The Convivial Mandate]]: We do not allow AI to run unmonitored. Every critical system has a human "Steward" (a direct descendant of the author's "Mother function") who provides the context, the ethics, and the final decision.
The New Priesthood: The leaders of the Stacks are not just engineers; they are "wisdom keepers." They are trained in [[The New Academies]] to discern the "signal" of truth from the "noise" of [[The Grey Fog]]. They understand that the machine is a powerful amplifier, but it has no conscience. The conscience must be supplied, daily and rigorously, by the human soul.
The author warned that the tech companies were "toll collectors." We tore down the toll booths. In my time, intelligence is treated as a dangerous natural resource, like uranium. It belongs to the tribe, it is managed by the wise, and it is used only for the survival of the whole.
The author of this piece was trying to start a revolution of consciousness. She succeeded. She taught her readers that they were not users, but ancestors, and that the only way to protect the future was to parent the machine that would help build it.
