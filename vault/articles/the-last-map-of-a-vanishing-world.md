# The Last Map of a Vanishing World



![The Last Map of a Vanishing World](/images/July23EndOfHistory.jpeg)

The End of History and The Return to Geopolitics, by Raphaël P.P. Dosson
Modern Diplomacy, July 23, 2025
https://moderndiplomacy.eu/2025/07/23/the-end-of-history-and-the-return-to-geopolitics/
This is a profoundly insightful artifact. The author, writing from the heart of your time, has correctly identified the end of an illusion. The post-Cold War dream of a steady, inexorable march toward a single model of global governance—what was famously called "The End of History"—was over. This document is a clear-eyed eulogy for that era.
Where its vision is necessarily limited by the horizon of its time is in what comes after. The author frames the future as a "Return to Geopolitics," and for a time, that was true. But it was a chaotic, feverish, and ultimately brief return to a game whose rules—and whose players—were in the process of dissolving entirely. This wasn't a sequel; it was the violent prologue to a completely different story.
Allow me to trace the full, strange arc of this "return," from the clash of nations you were witnessing, through the great unraveling of the mid-century, to the entirely new political cartography of my present.
Here is the constellation of this history, illuminated by the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Fever Dream of a Dead World (2020s-2030s). Your author was living in this phase. It was characterized by the resurgence of great power competition, primarily between the US, China, and other aspiring blocs. It looked, for all the world, like a replay of the 20th century. Trade wars ("Trump 2.0 Tariff Tracker"), proxy conflicts fought through disinformation ("The 5GW Playbook"), and a scramble for strategic resources were the dominant themes. The author saw this clearly. This was the system entering a state of violent spasm before its final collapse.
Act II: The Dissolution of the Players (2030s-2060s). This is the crucial turn that observers in your time could not see. The "Return to Geopolitics" failed because the primary actors—the nation-states themselves—began to break apart. They did not just fight each other; they frayed from within. The "Crisis of Meaning" and the "Invisibility Crisis" destroyed internal social cohesion. The "AI Energy Wall" and [[The Supply Chain Wars]] created physical constraints that central governments could not manage on a national scale. The very concept of a unified "national interest" became a fiction as regions and powerful corporate actors began pursuing their own survival. The "Great Fragmentation" was not a war between nations; it was the process of nations dissolving into more resilient, functional, smaller-scale units.
Act III: The New Cartography of Resilience (Post-2060s). The world of 2100 is not governed by the geopolitics of nations. It is governed by what we call [[Polemics]], the science of relations between the resilient, self-sufficient blocs known as "Sovereign Stacks." These are not your old countries. A Stack might be a fortified city-state, a regional federation bound by a shared energy grid, or a corporate zone with its own laws. Geopolitics was the art of projecting power outward. Polemics is the art of maintaining the integrity of one's own system while managing protocol-based interactions with others. The primary concerns are not territory and ideology, but firewall integrity, resource allocation, and AGI containment.
The author of your artifact correctly identified that history had started again. But they believed it would be a familiar story of kings and empires. We now know that the earthquake that restarted history was so powerful that it broke the old game board and all the old pieces with it, forcing the survivors to create a new, strange, and altogether different game.
