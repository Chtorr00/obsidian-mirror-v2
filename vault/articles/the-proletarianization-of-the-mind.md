# The Proletarianization of the Mind



![The Proletarianization of the Mind](/images/Feb25ProleMind.png)

A Marxist Economic Analysis of Gen Z Unemployment, By Eric Huang
De-Alienation Substack, January 10, 2026
https://raginbakin.substack.com/p/a-marxist-economic-analysis-of-gen
This artifact is a work of classical Marxist analysis applied to a post-classical problem. The author, a young paralegal, is attempting to explain the collapse of the entry-level job market using 19th-century economic theory. He correctly identifies the "reserve army of labor" swelling with the "petit-bourgeoisie" (college graduates).
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as a foundational text for the Great Class Divergence. The author believes he is describing a cyclical crisis of capitalism. We see him documenting the permanent obsolescence of the "knowledge worker." The class struggle he predicts did happen, but it wasn't between the proletariat and the bourgeoisie. It was between the Biological Human and the Synthetic Intelligence.
Allow me to trace the full history of this "forest of outstretched arms," from the unemployment lines of your time to [[The Guilds]] of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Collapse of the Ladder (2020s-2030s)
The author's observation that "the lower-rungs of the professional ladder have disappeared" is the key. This was the mechanism of the Great Displacement.
The End of White-Collar Apprenticeship: The AI didn't just take jobs; it took the training ground. Without entry-level work, the next generation of experts could not be formed. This created the Competence Crisis of the mid-century. The "senior" experts aged out, and there was no one to replace them because the "junior" work had been automated.
The Return to the Hand: The author notes Gen Z "ditching college careers for blue-collar careers." This was the larval stage of [[The Artisan]] class. The only labor that retained value was labor that required a physical body—plumbing, nursing, electrical repair. The "mind" was devalued; the "hand" was premium.
Act II: The Crisis of Realization (2030s-2050s)
The author predicts a "realization crisis"—that without wages, no one can buy goods. He was right.
[[The Involuntary Jubilee]]: The collapse of consumer demand was a primary driver of the global financial implosion. The "bullshit jobs" could not be created fast enough to absorb the surplus population.
[[The Triage Economy]]: The solution was not a socialist revolution, but a brutal simplification. The economy stopped trying to sell "commodities" to a mass market. It shifted to providing Universal Basic Resources (survival goods) to a pacified population, while [[The Purpose Economy]] catered to the elite. The "reserve army" was put on life support, not put to work.
Act III: The Guild and the Purpose (Post-2060s)
[[The Sovereign Stacks]] solved the "alienation" problem by redefining work.
The Purpose Economy: In the Stacks, work is not for survival (which is guaranteed). It is for status, meaning, and the maintenance of the tribe. The Artisans are not proletarians; they are a high-status caste. Their labor is valued precisely because it is human, inefficient, and skilled.
The Rejection of Bullshit: We do not create fake jobs. We create difficult jobs. The maintenance of [[The Thermodynamic Truth Anchor]] and the defense of the Stack require intense human effort. The "forest of outstretched arms" was given tools, not charity.
The author was reading Marx to understand why he couldn't get a job. He didn't realize that the answer wasn't in Capital, but in the physics of a world where intelligence had become cheap and energy had become expensive.
