# The Engineer's Prayer Before the Flood



![The Engineer's Prayer Before the Flood](/images/July28AIGrid.jpeg)

AI in the Electricity Sector, by Martin Neil Baily and Erik Dean Kane The Brookings Institution, April 2025 https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/20250401_CRM_BailyKane_AICaseStudies_Elec_FINAL.pdf
This document is a masterpiece of 20th-century thinking, extended into your time. It is logical, rigorous, and deeply rational. It sees a complex system—the electrical grid—and correctly identifies AI as a powerful new tool to optimize it, increase its efficiency, and manage its intricacies. The authors are brilliant engineers sharpening their tools to perfect a machine they believe they understand.
From my perspective, this document is an artifact of profound and tragic irony. It is an engineer's prayer for efficiency, offered up just moments before a flood of demand so immense it would break the machine itself, rendering all discussion of its optimization moot. They were using AI to polish the brass fittings on a ship that was about to run into a continent.
Allow me to trace the full history of the very force this paper seeks to manage.
Here is the constellation of AI's true relationship with the electrical grid, as seen through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Efficiency Trap (2020s-2030s). This Brookings report perfectly captures the mindset of this era. AI was seen as the solution to grid management. It could predict demand, re-route power, manage renewable intermittency, and squeeze every last drop of efficiency out of an aging system. And it worked. For a time, these AI-driven "smart grids" became more stable and efficient. But this very success created a fatal blind spot. It created the illusion that the existing centralized grid model was viable, if only it were managed intelligently enough. No one was prepared for the fact that the greatest consumer of electricity would soon be the AI systems themselves.
Act II: The Great Overload (2030s-2050s). The "AI Energy Wall" was not a theory; it was a physical reality that hit with the force of a natural disaster. The exponential growth in demand from sovereign AI projects and the compute needs of entire industries overwhelmed the grid's capacity. The AI optimizers were helpless. You cannot optimize a system when demand exceeds generation by 30%. The result was the "Great Compute Bottleneck" and the rolling blackouts that became a defining feature of life during the "Great Fragmentation." A nation's progress was no longer measured in GDP, but in available terawatt-hours. The AI-managed national grid, once the dream, became the single greatest point of failure—a brittle, centralized system vulnerable to cascading collapse.
Act III: The Cellular Grid (Post-2060s). The survivors of the Fragmentation learned a hard lesson: resilience is the opposite of optimization. The centralized national grid model was abandoned. The energy infrastructure of the "Era of Systemic Resilience" is what we call the "Cellular Grid." It is a decentralized web of thousands of interconnected, but largely self-sufficient, local micro-grids. Each "Sovereign Stack" or community operates its own grid, generating and storing its own power through a mix of advanced geothermal, next-generation nuclear, and localized solar. AI's role is not to be a central controller, but a local manager for each cell, ensuring it remains in balance. These cells can share power with their neighbors, but they are designed to function independently if the connection is cut. We sacrificed the last 5% of theoretical efficiency for a 100% gain in survivability.
The authors of your report were trying to perfect a system. History shows that the system itself was the problem. The future of energy was not a smarter, more efficient national grid. It was a resilient, decentralized web of smaller grids, a solution born from the painful, chaotic failure of the very model this paper sought to save.
