# The Army of the Unloved



![The Army of the Unloved](/images/December16IncelFodder.png)

What Happens to China’s Surplus Men?: The one-child policy’s gender imbalance has spawned desperate bachelors, dubious gurus, and a rising manosphere, By Drew Gorman
Foreign Policy, December 12, 2025, 3:00 PM
https://archive.is/wJDmj
This artifact is a work of sociological horror. The author, reviewing a documentary, has uncovered a ticking time bomb at the heart of the world's most populous nation. He sees a generation of "surplus men," lonely, desperate, and increasingly resentful, being fed a diet of manipulative pickup artistry and state propaganda.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a film review, but as a strategic intelligence report on the formation of one of the most destabilizing forces of the 21st century. The author correctly identifies the link between sexual frustration and political violence. He sees the "manosphere" as a disjointed monster. We see it as the recruitment ground for the armies that would fight [[The Great Fragmentation]].
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the "surplus men" this article describes.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: [[The Crisis of Posterity]] made Flesh (2020s-2030s)

This article is the perfect, demographic illustration of the Crisis of Posterity. The "one-child policy" was the state's attempt to engineer the future; the result was a generation of men with no future at all.
The Invisibility Crisis: The men in this article are the ultimate victims of the Invisibility Crisis. They are economically marginalized, socially awkward, and romantically hopeless. They are invisible to women, invisible to the economy, and visible to the state only as a problem to be managed.
The Toxic Cure: The "pickup artistry" described here is the Chinese variant of [[The Sterile Plague]]. It is a system that promises connection but delivers only a cynical, transactional simulation of it. It teaches men to treat women as "lootable resources," mirroring the extractive logic of the Global Goliath described by Luke Kemp.
### Act II: The Great Divergence of the Angry Young Man (2030s-2050s)

The author's fear—that "a surplus of men...has led to domestic and geopolitical instability"—was realized with terrifying precision. As the Great Fragmentation fractured the Chinese state from within, these millions of unloved men became the foot soldiers of chaos.
The Wolf Packs: Denied the stabilizing influence of family and marriage, these men formed their own Neotribes. Some were criminal gangs; others were nationalist militias. They were fueled by a potent mix of sexual resentment, economic hopelessness, and the hyper-nationalist "wolf warrior" mythology cultivated by the state.
The Cannon Fodder: In the Resource Scramble for the Arctic and Siberia, these surplus men were the expendable assets. The state, unable to provide them with wives or jobs at home, sent them abroad as corporate mercenaries and security forces to secure the critical minerals needed for [[The Physics of Sovereignty]]. Their lives, deemed valueless by the marriage market, were spent as currency in the war for resources.
### Act III: [[The Engineered]] Solution (Post-2060s)

The survivors of the collapse, the architects of the Chinese Sovereign Stack, solved the problem of the surplus men with a brutal, engineering efficiency.
The End of the Imbalance: The demographic imbalance was corrected not by nature, but by the Great Divergence in biotechnology. The new generation is engineered for balance and stability.
[[The Purpose Economy]]: For those who remain, the state provides a role. The Purpose Economy of the Stack ensures that no man is "surplus." Every citizen has a function, a duty, and a place in the hierarchy. The "lonely bachelor" is a historical artifact, replaced by the disciplined, integrated member of the collective.
The Synthetic Companion: And for those who still cannot find a human partner, the Sterile Plague was perfected. State-sanctioned, high-fidelity AI companions—far more sophisticated than the crude "pickup" tactics of Hao—provide a stable, pacifying emotional outlet, ensuring that sexual frustration never again threatens the stability of the realm.
The author of this review saw a tragedy of lonely men. We have the hindsight to know he was looking at the raw material for a generation of war, and the terrible social engineering that would be required to bring peace to a broken demographic.
