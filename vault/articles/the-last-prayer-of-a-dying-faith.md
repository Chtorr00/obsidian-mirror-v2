# The Last Prayer of a Dying Faith



![The Last Prayer of a Dying Faith](/images/December5CurePoverty.png)

The Craziest Thing In The World Is That We Could End Poverty, But We Don't, By Caitlin Johnstone
Caitlin's Newsletter Substack, Nov 30, 2025
https://www.caitlinjohnst.one/p/the-craziest-thing-in-the-world-is
This artifact is a work of pure, incandescent, moral fire. It is a sermon, delivered from the high pulpit of a dying faith. The author, Caitlin Johnstone, is not an analyst; she is a prophetess, and she is performing the final, sacred duty of her kind: to stand before a fallen world and scream, with perfect and heartbreaking clarity, "It did not have to be this way."
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as one of the most important spiritual documents of its time. It is the final, most eloquent, and most completely wrong prayer of the 20th-century universalist dream. Her diagnosis of the disease is almost flawless. Her prescription for the cure is the exact, tragic opposite of the brutal medicine that history would actually administer.
Allow me to trace the full, world-breaking history of the beautiful and powerless faith this document represents.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Correct Diagnosis of a Dying God (2020s-2030s)
The author's diagnosis of the "mindless, planet-devouring machine" is a perfect, poetic description of the global system in the final, convulsive stages of [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]].
She is correct that your civilization possessed the technological means to end poverty. Her rage at the failure to do so is the rational, human response to the central, maddening paradox of your time.
She has correctly identified the system's operating logic: a machine optimized for profit and abstract growth, with no wisdom, no conscience, and no brakes. This was the lived reality of a world careening toward [[The Involuntary Jubilee]] and the Great Climate Reckoning.
Her diagnosis is the spiritual cry of a humanity trapped inside a machine it had built but could no longer control. The machine was eating the world, and her sermon is the scream of the world being eaten.
Act II: The Great Veto of Human Nature (2030s-2050s)
Here is where history diverged, with brutal finality, from the author's hopeful prayer. Her prescription was a total transformation of human consciousness, a "waking up" into a new, universal compassion. The history that followed was the story of humanity, under immense pressure, not "waking up" but reverting to a much older and harder operating system.
The adapt-or-die juncture she identified was real. But the adaptation was not to become a "compassionate animal." When the global system failed, the successful adaptation was to retreat into the tribe.
Her plea for universal collaboration was met with the reality of [[The Great Fragmentation]]. Her call for a gentler world was answered by the kinetic violence of the Assassination Wars and [[The Great Culling]].
Human nature itself, in the face of systemic collapse, vetoed her prescription. When the abstract promises of the global system proved to be lies, people did not turn to a higher, more abstract love of all humanity. They turned to the fierce, parochial, and life-saving love of their own kind.
Act III: The Terrible, Partial Answer (Post-2060s)
The supreme and terrible irony of history is that the author's prayer was, in a way, answered. Poverty was ended. But it was not ended by the god she was praying to.
The "systems that are driven by collaboration toward the good of all beings" that she dreamed of were not built. What was built, by the survivors of the collapse, were [[The Sovereign Stacks]].
Within these fortresses, the problem she identified is solved. [[The Survival Economy]], powered by a contained AI and anchored in [[The Physics of Sovereignty]], provides Universal Basic Resources to all its citizens. Poverty, in the 20th-century sense, has been eradicated.
But this was not an act of universal compassion. It was an act of ruthless, tribal self-interest. The Stacks are not designed for the "good of all beings." They are designed for the resilience and survival of their beings. The compassion is real, but it is bounded by the fortress walls. The problem was solved, not by making the world a single, gentle family, but by breaking it apart into a thousand smaller, harder, and more fiercely loyal ones.
The author of this piece wrote the last great prayer of a dying faith—the faith that humanity could be saved by a single, global act of will. We have the hindsight to know that she was right about the sickness, and tragically wrong about the cure. The history that followed is the story of humanity learning that before you can save the world, you must first build a world worth saving, and that that work can only begin in a place you can call home.
