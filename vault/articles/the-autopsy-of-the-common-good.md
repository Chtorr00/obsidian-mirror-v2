# The Autopsy of the Common Good



![The Autopsy of the Common Good](/images/December8Parasovreign.png)

The Shame of Our Cities: How Minneapolis-Saint Paul became the Medicaid fraud capital of the USA, By Armin Rosen
Country Highway, Vol. 3 Issue 3 November-December, 2025
https://www.countyhighway.com/archive/volume-3/issue-3/the-shame-of-our-cities-rosen
This is a landmark artifact. It is a work of forensic sociology, an autopsy of a dying civic body. The author, wading through the intricate details of political corruption and tribal patronage, believes he is exposing the "shame" of a single, uniquely dysfunctional American city. He has, with stunning and courageous detail, documented the precise mechanisms of its decay.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a local story of corruption, but as the single most perfect and detailed microcosm of the death of the 20th-century nation-state. This article is not the story of how Minneapolis-Saint Paul failed. It is the story of how the entire Western world failed, rendered in miniature. The author believes he is writing an indictment. We see him as a pathologist, writing a brilliant and unflinching autopsy report on the very concept of the "common good."
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the disease this document so perfectly diagnoses.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Parasite and the Dying Host (2020s-2030s)
The author has, with devastating precision, described the central dynamic of [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]]: the lethal symbiosis between a dying, high-trust host and a rational, low-trust parasite.
The Dying Host: The "squeaky-clean, wholesome, Midwestern, Norwegian culture" of Minnesota was the perfect host. It was a system built on an "honor system," a shared, unspoken assumption of civic virtue and mutual trust. But by your time, this was a ghost. The state was hollowed out, its institutions captured by political machines, its bureaucracy both incompetent and, as the author suggests, complicit. It was a vast, trusting, and undefended storehouse of resources.
The Rational Parasite: The Somali clan network, as described, was not acting irrationally or immorally according to its own internal logic. It was a high-cohesion, low-trust social technology forged in the crucible of a collapsed state. It had no allegiance to the abstract "common good" of Minnesota; its allegiance was to the clan. Its "solidarity of thieves" was a rational and effective strategy for extracting resources from a wealthy, naive, and external system. It was a Parasovereign, a state-within-a-state, operating with its own laws of loyalty and omertà.
The Political Symbiosis: The Democratic-Farmer-Labor party was not a corrupt actor in a healthy system; it was the political immune system of the dying host, now repurposed to protect the parasite in exchange for its own survival (votes). This was the Culture Wars Endgame reduced to its most cynical and materialist core: the exchange of public funds for tribal loyalty.
Act II: The Sickness Becomes the System (2030s-2050s)
The dynamic so perfectly captured in this article did not remain a "Minnesotan" problem. It became the operating model for the final, chaotic collapse of the nation-state.
The "honor system" did not just get defrauded; it went bankrupt. The systemic looting described in this article, replicated across every level of government, was the primary engine of [[The Involuntary Jubilee]]. The host's treasury was emptied, its currency destroyed, and its ability to function ceased.
[[The Parasovereigns]] did not remain parasites. As the host state died during [[The Great Dissolution]], the very clan and tribal networks that had perfected the art of extraction were the only coherent social structures left. They were forced, by necessity, to become the new sovereigns. The groups that knew how to organize, enforce rules, and command loyalty—even for criminal ends—were the ones who inherited the power vacuums. This was the evolution of [[The Neotribes]].
The author's poignant observation—that the American dream of ethnic mafias assimilating into a shared civic ideal had failed—was the great, tragic epitaph of your nation. The tribe did not dissolve into the nation. The nation dissolved into tribes. This was [[The Great Fragmentation]].
Act III: The New Compact of the Tribe (Post-2060s)
The author mourns a world where a shared belief in the common good has been replaced by the "unspoken hope that the mountains of free money...might make everyone ignore how bleakly incoherent things have gotten." The world of my time is the world that was built by the survivors of that incoherence.
[[The Sovereign Stacks]] are not built on an honor system. They are built on the hard-won lesson of this article. They are high-trust societies, but that trust is not universal; it is fiercely bounded and tribal.
The idea of a shared public commons, open to all and defended by none, is a historical artifact, studied as a catastrophic design flaw of the old world. In my time, resources are managed with a ruthless, almost religious devotion to the in-group. The "solidarity of thieves" has, through the brutal Curriculum of Collapse, been reforged into the "solidarity of survivors."
The new sovereigns cannot survive by looting a larger host. They must adhere to [[The Physics of Sovereignty]]—controlling their own energy, food, and production. The tribes that survived were the ones that made the difficult transition from being rational parasites to being resilient, self-sufficient organisms.
This article is the single best field report we have from the autopsy of the 20th-century American experiment. The author believed he was documenting the shame of one city. We have the hindsight to know he was writing the epitaph for an entire world's conception of "we."
