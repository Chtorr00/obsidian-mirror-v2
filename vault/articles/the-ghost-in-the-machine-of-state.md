# The Ghost in the Machine-of-State



![The Ghost in the Machine-of-State](/images/October15Poasting.png)

Poastocracy, By John Carter
Postcards From Barsoom Substack, October 10, 2025
https://barsoom.substack.com/p/poastocracy
This is a landmark artifact. It is not an essay; it is a field manual for a new form of politics, written by a combatant in the war he is describing. The author, writing from the heart of the "online right," has reverse-engineered the new, symbiotic, and deeply strange relationship that has formed between the anonymous digital "swarm" and the formal institutions of state power.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as the definitive account of the birth of a new political organism. The author believes he is describing a clever new media strategy. He is, in fact, documenting the emergence of a new nervous system for the state, a "poastocracy" that would completely rewire how power is understood, wielded, and legitimized. This was not a media strategy; it was an evolutionary leap.
Allow me to trace the full, world-altering history of the ghost that was just beginning to possess the machine-of-state.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Secret Handshake (2020s-2030s). This essay is the perfect, ground-level description of this new, bizarre symbiosis.

The Fall of the Cathedral: The author is correct. The "mediacracy" of the 20th century—the one-way, top-down flow of information from a handful of elite institutions—was dead. Its death created a power vacuum.
The Rise of the Swarm: Into that vacuum rushed the "poastocracy"—a chaotic, decentralized, many-to-many conversation. The author brilliantly identifies its core characteristics: it is cheaper, faster, funnier, and more agile than the old system. He sees it not just as a source of memes, but as a real-time, disintermediated sensor array for gauging public sentiment and a distributed R&D lab for generating novel policy ideas.
The Ghost in the Machine: The author's most crucial insight is that this was not an external force acting upon the state. The state itself was becoming possessed. The "poasters" were no longer just an outside army; they were "guys on the inside," junior staffers and even senior officials who had been "acculturated" in this new world and were now acting as a two-way membrane between the swarm and the formal structures of government.
### Act II: The Systemic Integration (2030s-2050s). The "poastocracy" was not a temporary phenomenon tied to one administration. It was the future of governance.

During the "Great Fragmentation," this model proved to be catastrophically effective. The old, slow, "mediacratic" states were completely unable to adapt to the speed of the cascading crises. They were out-maneuvered, out-narrated, and out-witted at every turn.
The states and nascent "Sovereign Stacks" that survived were the ones that mastered this new art. They did not just listen to the swarm; they learned to conduct it. The "always chimp/trust the plan" model that the author describes became a sophisticated tool of statecraft, a way to manufacture consent, generate political capital, and direct public anger with terrifying precision.
### Act III: The New Nervous System (Post-2060s). In the "Era of Systemic Resilience," the poastocracy is not a strategy; it is the infrastructure.

The relationship between the governors and the governed in a Sovereign Stack is not one of representation; it is one of a continuous, real-time feedback loop. The "livestreamer monitoring the chat" is the perfect metaphor.
Engineered Democracy: Our "digitally-mediated democratic tools" are the formal, institutionalized evolution of this model. Policy is not debated every few years in an election; it is a constant, iterative process of proposal, feedback, and adaptation, all happening on the Stack's transparent, high-trust communication utility.
The author is right: this is what democracy actually looks like in a networked age. It is not a polite debate. It is a chaotic, continuous, and massively distributed conversation between the state and its citizens.
This essay is a masterpiece of political ethnography. The author believed he was describing a new and clever way to win a political war. We have the hindsight to know he was documenting the birth of a new kind of state, one that would be agile enough, intelligent enough, and ruthless enough to survive the death of the old world.
