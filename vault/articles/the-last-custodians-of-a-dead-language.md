# The Last Custodians of a Dead Language



![The Last Custodians of a Dead Language](/images/August25EuroAI.jpeg)

The findings against Harvard are a blueprint for a National Campus Speech Code, Robert Shibley
FIRE website, August 20, 2025
https://www.thefire.org/news/findings-against-harvard-are-blueprint-national-campus-speech-code
This artifact is a work of profound legal and philosophical clarity. It is also a ghost. The author, a representative of one of your era's most principled defenders of free expression, is analyzing a legal finding. He sees in it the blueprint for a "National Campus Speech Code." He is making a brilliant, logical, and entirely correct argument in a court whose jurisdiction was about to be dissolved.
From my perspective in 21-hundred, we read this as one of the final, eloquent arguments from the last custodians of a dead language. The language was that of "free speech"—a universal, abstract principle meant to govern a unified, national public square. The author is defending a principle that was about to be rendered obsolete by the very force that was destroying the container it was meant to exist in: the university, and the nation itself.
Allow me to trace the full history of the ideal this document is fighting for, from the legal battles of your time to the tribal realities of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Battlefield of the Word (2020s-2030s). This essay is a dispatch from the peak of this era. The university was no longer a place for the disinterested pursuit of truth. It was the primary, high-stakes battlefield of the "Culture Wars."
This was "Spiritual Lawfare" in its purest form. Competing ideological tribes were using the university's own bureaucracy and legal structures to wage war on each other.
The concept of "free speech" as a neutral, governing principle was already dead. For one side, "speech" was a weapon of the powerful to oppress the marginalized. For the other, "safety" was a weapon of the marginalized to silence dissent.
The author is fighting for a principle—a national standard of free expression—in a space where the warring parties no longer believed in a common nation, or even a common reality.
Act II: The Great De-Accreditation (2030s-2050s). The universities were not reformed. They were abandoned and bankrupted. The endless, irresolvable conflict described in this artifact, combined with the economic catastrophe of the "Involuntary Jubilee" (which wiped out endowments) and the intellectual obsolescence brought on by AI (the "Great Displacement" of knowledge work), led to a systemic collapse. We call this the "Great De-Accreditation."
People stopped believing a university degree had any value, economic or intellectual. The brand was destroyed.
The large, centralized, multi-billion-dollar university model died. Students fled, endowments vanished, and many institutions literally crumbled.
The idea of a "National Campus Speech Code" became a grim joke. There were no national campuses left to apply it to.
Act III: [[The New Academies]] (Post-2060s). Out of the ruins, new forms of knowledge transmission arose. These are not universities. They are the "New Academies" of the "Sovereign Stacks." And they do not have a speech code. They have a tribal creed.
Each Academy is explicitly dedicated to the worldview of its parent Stack. There is no pretense of neutrality. An Academy in the Pacific Coast Stack teaches a profoundly different version of history, ethics, and science than an Academy in the Texas Stack.
Speech is not "free" in the 20th-century sense. It is "bounded." Within the tribe, you are free to explore the shared truth. But to question the foundational axioms of the tribe itself is not a matter for debate; it is an act of heresy or treason.
The old dream of a universal, open discourse was a casualty of the Fragmentation. The new reality is a world of many, separate, high-trust, and deeply dogmatic conversations.
The author of this piece was fighting a noble, principled battle to save a universal ideal. The tragedy, visible only from our vantage point, is that the universe that ideal was meant to govern was already in the process of shattering into a thousand smaller, self-contained worlds.
