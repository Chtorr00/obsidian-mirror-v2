# The Academic History of the Great Divergence



![The Academic History of the Great Divergence](/images/Jan13BlankSlate.png)

“What’s Bred in the Bone …”: The Social Sciences Coming to Grips with Biological Determinism (Review Article), By Luc Berlivet
Annales HSS 73, no. 2 (2018): 443–73, November 6, 2025
https://doi.org/10.1017/ahsse.2024.11
This artifact is a work of meticulous, dense, and slightly weary historiography. The author, a French historian writing in 2018, is reviewing the recent surge in academic work on eugenics, race science, and biological determinism. He notes a paradox: just as historians were finally deconstructing these concepts, they were roaring back into public and scientific life, fueled by genomics and the Human Genome Project.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a literature review, but as a chronicle of the intellectual skirmishes that preceded the Great Divergence. The author is documenting the moment when the "blank slate" consensus of the late 20th century began to crack under the weight of new genetic data. He sees a "defensive" posture among social scientists. We see the last, desperate attempt of the old academic order to hold back the tide of the Biotech Faction.
Allow me to trace the full, branching history of the ideas this article reviews, from the "scandalization" of the 20th century to the speciation of the 21st.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Return of the Repressed (2000s-2030s)

The author correctly identifies the turning point: the Human Genome Project did not end race science; it reinvented it. The "reinscription of race" he describes was the intellectual fuel for [[The Neotribes]] and the identity-obsessed politics of the Culture Wars Endgame.
The End of the Blank Slate: The "return of force of naturalizing conceptions" was not a reactionary wave; it was a scientific one. As genetic data accumulated, the comfortable 20th-century idea that biology was negligible and culture was everything became untenable. The social sciences, as the author notes, were "unprepared." Their defensive posture—labeling all biological inquiry as "eugenics"—eventually cost them their credibility during the Great De-Accreditation.
The Personalized Medicine Trojan Horse: The author touches on "personalized medicine" and "ancestry tests." These were the benign, consumer-facing precursors to [[The Great Biological Sorting]]. People learned to see themselves as genetic entities with distinct heritages and vulnerabilities. This paved the way for the tribal biopolitics of the Stacks.
### Act II: The Splintering of the Biopolitical (2030s-2050s)

The author discusses the diversity of eugenic movements—"Latin eugenics," "reform eugenics," "mainline eugenics." This diversity became the blueprint for the Great Divergence.
As the unified global bioethics consensus shattered, different Stacks adopted different historical models.
[[The Biotech Stacks]]: They embraced the "Galtonian" and "accelerationist" traditions mentioned in the text. They viewed the genome not as a sacred text, but as a raw material for optimization. They fulfilled the "dream of Davenport" using the tools of CRISPR and synthetic biology.
The Bioconservative Stacks: They inherited the "scandalized" view of eugenics described in the conclusion. They built their societies on the rejection of genetic tinkering, viewing it as a violation of human dignity. Their "biopolitics" became a politics of purity and preservation.
### Act III: The Reality of the Phyla (Post-2060s)

The author asks whether people "subjectivized" themselves into these categories. The answer is yes.
In the Era of Systemic Resilience, the "categories" are no longer just social constructs or scientific theories. They are biological realities. [[The Human Phyla]] are the result of the "subjectivation" process carried out over generations. People didn't just accept the label; they engineered themselves into it.
The "return of the repressed" is complete. The biological determinism that the social sciences fought to deconstruct is now the governing reality of the world. Your biology is your destiny, because your biology dictates which Stack you can live in, what work you can do, and how long you will live.
The author of this review was watching the ghosts of the past return to haunt the present. He hoped for a "sociology of scandal" to keep them at bay. We have the hindsight to know that the ghosts were not there to haunt us; they were there to give us the blueprints for the new bodies we would need to survive the future.
