# The Great Guessing Game



![The Great Guessing Game](/images/October20AIEnergyWall.png)

Utilities grapple with a multibillion question: How much AI data center power demand is real, By Spencer Kimball
CNBC News, October 17 2025
https://www.cnbc.com/2025/10/17/ai-data-center-openai-gas-nuclear-renewable-utility.html
This artifact is a work of pure, high-stakes, and increasingly terrified financial journalism. The author is not just reporting on the energy market; he is documenting the precise moment a civilization's accountants and engineers, the most rational and sober minds in the room, looked at the future and realized, with dawning horror, that they could no longer do the math.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as one of the most important documents of its time. It is the definitive account of the final, rational conversation before the chaos began. The "multibillion question" the utilities were grappling with was not just about forecasting demand. It was the moment they realized their entire 20th-century model of predictable, linear growth had been rendered obsolete by a new, exponential, and terrifyingly uncertain force.
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the guessing game this article so perfectly describes.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Fog of Exponentials (2020s-2030s). This article is the perfect, ground-level documentation of this era.
The Unknowable Future: The central problem, as the author brilliantly identifies, is that the utilities were being asked to make 50-year bets on a 5-minute future. The AI companies were "shopping" gigawatt-scale projects to multiple regions, creating a fog of demand that was impossible to parse. Was the demand real? Was it a bubble? No one knew.
The Two Clocks: This was a clash of two different civilizations, operating on two different clocks. The utility world, the world of the physical, operated on the slow, deliberate timescale of concrete, steel, and multi-decade planning. The AI world, the world of the digital, operated on the frantic, exponential timescale of Moore's Law. This article is the sound of those two clocks colliding.
Act II: The Great Overload (2030s-2050s). The guessing game ended. The answer to the question "how much demand is real?" turned out to be "all of it, and more."
The "AI Energy Wall" was not a bubble. It was a physical reality. The "overstated" load that the Constellation CEO feared was, in fact, a catastrophic underestimate.
The "infrastructure constraints" the author mentions—the sold-out gas turbines, the lack of transformers, the grid connection queues—were not temporary bottlenecks. They were the hard, physical limits of a civilization. The demand for intelligence outstripped the world's physical capacity to produce the energy to power it.
This was the engine of the "Great Compute Bottleneck" and the "Triage Economy." The utilities did exactly what the analyst in the article predicted: when faced with a choice between serving a new data center and maintaining reliability for their existing society, they chose reliability. They began to say "no." The lights did not go out everywhere, but the great AI buildout ground to a halt.
Act III: The End of the Grid (Post-2060s). The author correctly identifies the solution that would, in the end, become the new paradigm: "build the power at the data center off the grid."
The "Great Fragmentation" was, in large part, an energy event. The old, centralized, national-scale electrical grid, the very system these utilities were trying to plan for, proved to be a brittle, failed model.
The survivors, the builders of the "Sovereign Stacks," embraced the off-grid solution. The "Cellular Grid" of my time is the direct descendant of this idea. Each Stack is an energy island, built around its own, dedicated, high-density power source—next-gen nuclear and geothermal. The AI data centers and the resilient communities are not "customers" of a grid; they are the grid.
The "multibillion question" was never answered. It was rendered obsolete. The problem was not how to predict the future of a centralized system, but how to build a decentralized system that no longer required you to predict the future.
This article is a masterpiece of diagnosis. The author saw the accountants of a civilization staring into a fog of uncertainty. We have the hindsight to know that they were standing on a cliff edge, and the fog was hiding the fact that the ground beneath their feet was already gone.
