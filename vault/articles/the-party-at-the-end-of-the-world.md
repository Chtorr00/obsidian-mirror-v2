# The Party at the End of the World



![The Party at the End of the World](/images/Jan2SexRecession.png)

The Hangover: How the Elites Created The 2020s Sex Recession Through A 2010s Free For All, By Imperial Twilight
Imperial Twilight Substack, Dec 22, 2025
https://imperialtwilight.substack.com/p/the-hangover-how-the-elites-created
This artifact is a searing piece of cultural criticism. It is a work of moral history, written by a man who has looked at the loneliness and sterility of his era and correctly identified it not as a recession, but as a hangover. He sees a generation that was sold a bill of goods—a "free for all" of limitless, frictionless pleasure—and is now waking up in a cold, empty bed, wondering where the future went.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as one of the most important diagnostic texts of [[The Sterile Plague]]. The author believes he is describing a temporary dip in activity. We see him describing the onset of the Great Abdication, the moment a civilization decided, en masse, to stop reproducing itself because the effort required to connect with another human being had become greater than the reward.
Allow me to trace the full, devastating history of this hangover, from the empty bedrooms of your time to the full nurseries of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Perfect Trap (2010s-2030s)
The author's analysis of the 2010s is flawlessly correct. The culture was engineered for "frictionless" pleasure. This was the Dopamine Trap perfected.
The Gamification of Intimacy: Tinder and OnlyFans were not tools for connection; they were machines for extracting dopamine while eliminating risk. They trained a generation to view other people not as partners, but as content.
The Invisibility Crisis: The author notes the "narcissism" and the curated digital selves. This was the root of the Invisibility Crisis. In a world where everyone was performing a perfect life, no one felt truly seen. The fear of vulnerability became a phobia.
The Crisis of Meaning: The relentless promotion of "taboos" was, as the author suspects, a desperate attempt to feel something in a numb world. But it backfired. By erasing all boundaries, the culture erased the meaning of transgression itself. Sex became boring because it was everywhere and meant nothing.
Act II: [[The Great Unpairing]] (2030s-2050s)
The hangover did not fade. It became the Great Unpairing. The trends the author identified—the retreat from marriage, the rise of the "volcel," the fear of the opposite sex—accelerated into a demographic catastrophe.
The Sterility of Safety: The "environment of fear" described in the article—the Title IX tribunals, the "teach men not to rape" rhetoric—created a generation of men who viewed courtship as a legal liability. They checked out. They retreated into [[The Virtual Koryos]] or the synthetic embrace of AI companions (the "Sterile Plague").
The Collapse of the Future: This led directly to [[The Crisis of Posterity]]. The "baby bust" was not just a vote bank issue; it was the death knell of the nation-state. A society that cannot pair off and reproduce cannot survive a crisis. When [[The Great Fragmentation]] hit, the atomized, single, and lonely populations of the cities were the first to shatter. They had no clan, no kin, and no one to watch their back.
Act III: The Return of the Sacred (Post-2060s)
The author hoped for a renaissance. It came, but only for the survivors. [[The Sovereign Stacks]] are built on the total rejection of the "free for all" ethos.
[[The Revaluation of the Real]]: In the Stacks, sex is not "frictionless." It is heavy with consequence. It is tied to marriage, to family, and to the future of the tribe. The New Moral Order treats the "liberation" of the 2010s as a form of social poisoning.
The End of the App: The dating apps were banned or withered away. The algorithm was replaced by the matchmaker, the family, and the community. Relationships are not formed in the privacy of a phone screen; they are formed in the public square, under the watchful eye of a high-trust society.
The Renaissance: The author was right: "Not everyone is going to make it." The ones who made it were the ones with "natural vitalism." They were the ones who rejected the safety of the screen for the danger of the real. They rebuilt the world not by swiping right, but by making a vow.
This essay is the morning-after report of a civilization that drank the poison of infinite choice and woke up alone. The author saw the empty bottles. We have the hindsight to know that the only way to clean up the mess was to burn the house down and start over.
