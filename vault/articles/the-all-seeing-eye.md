# The All-Seeing Eye



![The All-Seeing Eye](/images/September27Palantir.png)

Palantir Software Gotham – Background and Description, By Thomas Franke
vernetztesicherheit.de, August 26, 2025
https://vernetztesicherheit.de/palantir-software-background-and-description/
This is a landmark artifact. It is a work of deep, principled, and terrified philosophical inquiry, disguised as a technology review. The author is not just describing a piece of software; he is staring into the crystal ball—the Palantir—of a new and terrifying form of state power, and he is trying, with all the tools of his liberal, democratic tradition, to understand the ghost he sees within it.
From my perspective in 2100, we see this document as one of the final, and most eloquent, warnings from the old world about the nature of the world that was coming. The author is grappling with the question of whether a society can use a tool of totalizing surveillance and remain free. The history that followed answered his question with a brutal and unambiguous "no."
Allow me to trace the full, world-altering history of the all-seeing eye this document describes.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Seductive Solution (2020s-2030s). This essay is a perfect dispatch from this era.
The Unspoken Bargain: The author is right: despite the fierce criticism from civil liberties groups, the software was being adopted at a frantic pace across Europe. Why? Because the states of the "Age of Institutional Exhaustion" were terrified. They were facing a rising tide of crime, terrorism, and social unrest they could no longer control with 20th-century methods. Palantir's Gotham offered a seductive bargain: give us your data, and we will give you order.
The Philosophical Trojan Horse: The author's deepest insight is to connect the software to the explicitly anti-universalist, anti-liberal philosophy of its creator, Alexander Karp. This was not just a tool; it was a worldview, encoded in software. It was a Trojan horse carrying a philosophy of order and tribal security into the heart of the liberal state.
Act II: The Instrument of the Culling (2030s-2050s). The author worries about "mission creep." This is a profound understatement. During the chaos of the "Great Fragmentation," Gotham and its successors did not just creep; they became the central nervous system of the state's desperate war for survival.
The Unseen Enemy: As the "Assassination Wars" and "[[The Great Culling]]" began, the enemy was no longer a terrorist cell, but a diffuse, anonymous network of domestic radicals, operating in the shadows. The only tool that could "see" this new enemy was the all-seeing eye of the data-aggregation state.
The End of Due Process: The author notes the difficulty of using Gotham's outputs in a court of law. This problem was "solved" in the most brutal way possible. During the deepest crises, the state abandoned the pretense of the court. The system's outputs were no longer "investigative leads"; they were kill orders. The system did not need to be "court-proof" when it was being used to direct drone swarms, not prosecutors.
Act III: [[The Architecture of Pervasive Security]] (Post-2060s). The critics in your time were right. The technology was not compatible with a free and open society. The society changed. [[The Sovereign Stacks]] of my era are the direct, logical endpoint of the world that Palantir's software helped to build.
[[The Watchful Peace]]: The "trusted advisor" that the author questions is now the permanent, silent partner in the governance of every Stack. The "Architecture of Pervasive Security" is the foundational guarantee of the internal, high-trust cohesion of the Stacks.
The Death of Anonymity: The very concept of privacy, as your era understood it, is a historical artifact. Within a Stack, all data is, by default, integrated into the system. Anonymity is not a right; it is a signal of a threat.
The author ends with the hope that we might choose a good larger version of the world, not a dystopia. The terrible, and perhaps necessary, irony is that the world that survived is both. The Stacks are stable, prosperous, and safe for their citizens. And they achieved this by becoming the very thing the author feared: a society of total, and totalizing, information awareness.
This essay is the sound of a free man wrestling with the price of order. The history that followed is the story of a civilization that, faced with a choice between freedom and survival, chose survival. And built a beautiful, glittering, and inescapable cage.
