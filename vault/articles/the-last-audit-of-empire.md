# The Last Audit of Empire



![The Last Audit of Empire](/images/Decemeber29USGFranchise.png)

Trump’s geo-politics: Correcting the imperial model & shaping the incoming economic architecture, By Alastair Crooke
Strategic Culture Foundation, December 17, 2025
https://strategic-culture.su/news/2025/12/17/trumps-geo-politics-correcting-the-imperial-model-shaping-the-incoming-economic-architecture/
This artifact is a work of geopolitical autopsy. The author, a strategic analyst, is reading the entrails of the National Security Strategy and the policies of the Trump administration, and correctly diagnosing them not as a plan for a new world order, but as the bankruptcy proceedings for the old one. He sees the "business model" of the empire shifting from hegemony to "franchising," from free trade to a protectionist fortress.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as one of the most lucid early descriptions of the Great Shortening and the formation of the North American Sovereign Stack. The author believes he is analyzing a shift in US foreign policy. We know he is witnessing the geopolitical equivalent of a star collapsing into a black hole—the contraction of a global empire into a fortified regional singularity.
Allow me to trace the full, kinetic history of this "business model," from the tariffs of your time to the fortress walls of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Franchise Model (2020s-2030s)

The author's "franchise" analogy is brilliant and precise. The United States, realizing it could no longer be the global policeman, attempted to outsource security to regional managers (the "burden-shifting" of the NSS).
This was the logic behind the "Trump Corollary": consolidate the core (North America), extract rent from the periphery (franchise fees), and abandon the rest to chaos.
The rejection of the Venezuelan oil deal was not a mistake; it was the first application of [[The Physics of Sovereignty]]. The US didn't want oil (which was fungible); it wanted control over the architecture of extraction. It couldn't allow a Chinese-built infrastructure to exist within its fortress, even if it meant leaving oil in the ground. This was the beginning of [[The Supply Chain Wars]] turning kinetic.
### Act II: The Failure of Coercion (2030s-2050s)

The fatal flaw in the "shakedown" strategy was that the US no longer had the leverage to enforce it. The "gaslighting" required to sell US debt failed spectacularly in [[The Involuntary Jubilee]].
The "franchise fees" stopped coming. Europe, hollowed out by deindustrialization and internal strife, could not pay for its own defense, let alone pay tribute to Washington. The "outsourcing" of security collapsed as the proxy managers were overwhelmed by [[The Great Fragmentation]].
The "naval blockade" of Venezuela was the last gasp of 20th-century gunboat diplomacy. In a world of decentralized finance and alternative payment rails (the Chinese system), a physical blockade was a leaky sieve. The "war for the incoming economic architecture" was won by the architecture that didn't rely on a single, failing point of failure (the dollar).
### Act III: The Closed Loop (Post-2060s)

The author's prediction of a "regional fortress" was realized, but not as an empire. It became the North American Sovereign Stack.
The "Hamiltonian" vision of a protected, industrial economy was achieved, but only after the global system had completely disintegrated. The Stack is a Closed-Loop Economy, autarkic and self-sufficient, not because it won a trade war, but because trade became impossible.
The "Trump Corollary" evolved into [[The Architecture of Pervasive Security]]. The Stack does not just "push out" foreign companies; it maintains a total, hermetically sealed perimeter. The "competition" with China is no longer about who shapes the global system (there isn't one), but about whose fortress can best withstand the entropy of the world outside.
The author was watching an empire trying to downsize into a business. We have the hindsight to know that the business would eventually go bankrupt, and the only assets that could be saved were the ones behind the walls of the fortress it built in the liquidation sale.
