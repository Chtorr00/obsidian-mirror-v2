# The Illusion of the 'Correct' Scroll



![The Illusion of the 'Correct' Scroll](/images/August22UBIwoes.jpeg)

The Double-Edged Scroll: Active vs. Passive Social Media Use and Stress, by Pathum B. Rathnayake
Information Matters, February 4, 2025
https://informationmatters.org/2025/02/the-double-edged-scroll-active-vs-passive-social-media-use-and-stress/
This artifact is a work of remarkable precision for its time. It is a dispatch from deep within the psychic landscape of your era, written by an author who is attempting to draw a map of his own prison cell. The distinction he makes—between "active" and "passive" social media use—is an entirely correct and logical analysis of the different ways the system could inflict stress.
From my perspective in 2100, we see this as a perfect, and perfectly tragic, example of a civilization trying to find the "correct" way to live inside a system that was, by its very design, fundamentally hostile to human flourishing. The author was asking how to scroll. The history that followed proved that this question was the trap.
Allow me to trace the full history of the prison cell this author was so accurately mapping.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Perfectly Calibrated Prison (2020s-2030s). This essay captures the core dilemma of the "Shattered Focus" crisis. The author is correct: the scroll was a double-edged sword.

Passive Use (The Void): To passively consume was to fall into the "Invisibility Crisis." It was to be a ghost in the machine, watching a performance of life that you were not a part of, leading to feelings of envy, loneliness, and alienation.
Active Use (The Arena): To actively participate was to step into a brutal arena. It was to subject yourself to the judgment of the algorithm and the crowd, leading to performance anxiety, the stress of online conflict, and the exhaustion of constant self-branding.
The system was a perfectly calibrated prison for the mind. If you were silent, it made you feel worthless. If you spoke, it made you feel exhausted and attacked. The search for a "healthy balance" between these two states was the defining, and ultimately unwinnable, struggle for a generation.
### Act II: The Uninhabitable Space (2030s-2050s). The distinction between active and passive use eventually became moot, for a simple reason: the space itself became uninhabitable.

During the "Great Fragmentation," the major social platforms were not just stressful; they became the primary battlegrounds for "5GW" (Fifth-Generation Warfare). They were no longer places for social connection, but vectors for state-sponsored psychological operations, extremist recruitment, and social sabotage.
The ambient stress this author describes was replaced by acute, targeted psychological trauma. It was no longer a question of feeling bad; it was a question of being actively harmed.
The result was not reform. It was the "Great Digital Exodus." People fled the global platforms en masse, not because they found a "better way to scroll," but because the city they were living in was on fire.
### Act III: The Architecture of Presence (Post-2060s). The survivors of the Exodus did not try to build a "better" social media. They built a different category of tool altogether, based on a different philosophy. These are the "Communication Utilities" of my era.

Their primary design goal is not to maximize "use" (active or passive), but to facilitate real-world, embodied presence.
They are not designed as a "place" to be, but as a quiet, reliable tool to organize your life in the physical world. Think of them less as a glittering casino designed to keep you inside, and more as a clear, simple signpost designed to help you get where you are actually going.
The distinction between active and passive use dissolves, because the tool's success is measured by how quickly and efficiently it can help you close the application and go and do something with another human being in the real world.
The author of your artifact was engaged in a noble and intelligent effort to find the least harmful path through a toxic landscape. The history that followed is the story of humanity learning that the only winning move was to abandon that landscape entirely and build a new world on healthier ground.
