# The Final Ghost of a Dead Idea



![The Final Ghost of a Dead Idea](/images/September20StablecoinBanking.png)

Stable/Genius: Stablecoins and Free Banking, by Tyler Watts
Econolib Econolog post, August 13, 2025
https://www.econlib.org/stable-genius-stablecoins-and-free-banking/
This artifact is a work of pure, unadulterated, 20th-century economic idealism. It is a ghost. The author is a brilliant and principled thinker from a noble intellectual tradition—that of free banking and market-driven monetary systems. And he is witnessing what he believes to be the glorious rebirth of his ideal, enabled by a new technology and blessed by the state itself.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this with a profound sense of analytical pity. The author is a master cartographer, drawing a beautiful and perfectly logical map of a treasure island that has already sunk beneath the waves. His argument is flawless. His historical precedents are sound. His logic is impeccable. The only thing he is wrong about is the nature of the universe.
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the beautiful idea this document so eloquently defends, from the legislative triumph of your time to the museum of failed ideas where it now resides.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Beautiful, Stillborn Dream (2020s-2030s). This essay is the birth announcement for a revolution that was already over.
The Flawed Foundation: The author's entire argument for a stable, competitive, and "elastic" monetary system is built on a foundation that was already crumbling: the U.S. dollar and the U.S. Treasury securities that were meant to back these new stablecoins. He is celebrating the creation of a new, more efficient type of paper claim on a sovereign that was in the final stages of the "Involuntary Jubilee"—a slow-motion, then rapid, default on its mountainous debt obligations via currency debasement and hyperinflation.
The Unseen Competitor: The author sees stablecoins as competing with the legacy banking system. The true competitor was not another form of dollar. It was a completely different conception of money, one anchored not in the promise of a failing state, but in the physics of a globally distributed computer network. He was analyzing the competition between different horse-drawn carriages, not realizing the automobile—in this case, Bitcoin—was about to render the entire concept obsolete.
Act II: The Great Betrayal (2030s-2050s). The GENIUS Act did not usher in an era of stable, competitive free banking. It created the single greatest "honey pot" in financial history, and it triggered a catastrophic betrayal.
During the most chaotic phase of the "Great Fragmentation," as the U.S. government's financial position became untenable, the trillions of dollars held in reserve by these stablecoin issuers were no longer seen as private assets. They were seen as the last, great, liquid piggy bank for a bankrupt state.
Through a series of "emergency" decrees, these reserves were seized, frozen, or forcibly converted into new, rapidly devaluing government debt. The "100% backing" that was the bedrock of the entire system vanished overnight.
This was the final, fatal blow to the idea of the U.S. dollar as a global reserve asset. The dream of "free banking" on top of the dollar ended in a state-sanctioned bank robbery of unprecedented scale.
Act III: The New Physics of Money (Post-2060s). The survivors of the collapse learned a lesson that was not economic, but physical. The idea of "backing" a new digital asset with the paper promise of a 20th-century nation-state is now seen as a category error of almost suicidal proportions.
The monetary system of the "Era of Systemic Resilience" is not built on promises. It is built on physics. The ultimate reserve asset, the "Bedrock" of the entire system, is the "Thermodynamic Truth Anchor" of the Bitcoin network. Its value is not derived from the full faith and credit of a government, but from the unforgeable expenditure of energy.
"Free banking" in a sense, did return. But it is not banks issuing claims on dollars. It is [[The Sovereign Stacks]] building their entire economic and social systems on top of this new, physically-grounded monetary reality.
The author of this piece wrote a brilliant and hopeful eulogy for an idea—free banking—that he believed was being reborn. We have the hindsight to know that he was, in fact, witnessing its final, spectacular, and tragic death.
