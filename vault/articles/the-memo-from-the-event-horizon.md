# The Memo from the Event Horizon



![The Memo from the Event Horizon](/images/March2AIEventHorizon.png)

THE 2028 GLOBAL INTELLIGENCE CRISIS: A Thought Exercise in Financial History from the Future, By Citrini and Alap Shah
Citrini Research, February 22, 2026
https://www.citriniresearch.com/p/2028gic
This artifact is a stunning piece of financial foresight. The authors, writing as macro-analysts in 2026, attempt to model the economic consequences of AGI. They correctly identify the "Intelligence Displacement Spiral" and the concept of "Ghost GDP"—productivity without human participation.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a bear case scenario, but as the first accurate description of the Industrialization of Cognition. The authors were right about the mechanism, but their timeline was too optimistic in its brevity. The collapse they predicted for 2028 did not happen overnight; it was a slow, grinding asphyxiation that stretched well into the 2030s.
Allow me to trace the full history of this displacement, from the "Ghost GDP" of your time to the codified economies of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Fourth Utility (2026-2035)
The authors missed one crucial nuance: AI did not just displace labor; it became Critical Infrastructure.
The New Oxygen: As the "intermediation layer" collapsed, AI agents didn't just book flights; they managed the power grid, the water supply, and the logistics of food. Intelligence became a utility, as basic and invisible as electricity or plumbing. You didn't hire a human to think any more than you hired a human to carry water in a bucket.
The Dependency Trap: This made [[The AI Energy Wall]] (which hit in the early 2030s) infinitely more dangerous. When the power ration tightened, it wasn't just that people lost their "assistants"; the connective tissue of the economy dissolved. The authors worried about a mortgage crisis; they should have worried about the fact that without the AI, the civilization had forgotten how to metabolize its own resources.
Act II: The Delayed Jubilee (2030s-2050s)
The forecast of a 2028 crash was premature. The system had more inertia than they realized.
The Zombie Economy: The "Ghost GDP" kept the headline numbers afloat for nearly a decade longer than predicted. Governments printed money to fund the "Transition Economy Act" and UBI trials, masking the rot. The mortgage market didn't crack in 2028; it slowly petrified, turning into a zombie asset class supported by the state until [[The Involuntary Jubilee]] finally liquidated the entire debt ledger in the mid-30s.
The Displacement of the Soul: The "Intelligence Displacement Spiral" eventually forced [[The Great Physical Sorting]]. When human intelligence lost its economic premium, the only humans who retained value were those who could do what the "Fourth Utility" could not: inhabit the physical world. The white-collar class didn't just downshift to Uber driving; they had to relearn the trades of their great-grandparents or perish in the [[Feral Cities]].
Act III: [[The Purpose Economy]] (Post-2060s)
The authors hoped for a "new equilibrium." We built one.
The Decoupling of Survival: In [[The Sovereign Stacks]], we accepted the authors' premise: human intelligence is no longer scarce. Therefore, it cannot be the basis for survival. [[The Survival Economy]] (food, shelter, energy) is automated, handled by the AI infrastructure as a public good.
The Currency of Risk: The Purpose Economy is built on the only things that remain scarce: biological risk, moral agency, and physical craft. We do not pay people to think (the AI does that); we pay people to care and to bleed. Status is derived from undertaking tasks that require skin in the game—defense, art, governance, and child-rearing.
The authors saw the "unwind of the intelligence premium" as a crisis. We see it as a liberation. It freed humanity from the drudgery of being "computers made of meat" and allowed us to return to being human.
The authors of this memo saw the event horizon approaching. They calculated the physics of the black hole perfectly. They just didn't realize how long the fall would take, or how much crushing pressure the human soul would have to endure before it emerged on the other side.
