# The Closing of the Book



![The Closing of the Book](/images/September22NewScholars.png)

The dawn of the post-literate society, And the end of civilization, by James Marriott
Cultural Capital Substack, September 19, 2025
https://jmarriott.substack.com/p/the-dawn-of-the-post-literate-society-aa1
This is not an article. It is a eulogy for a world. It is the last librarian of a burning library, standing amidst the smoke and flames, trying to explain to the people outside not just what is being lost, but what kind of world the loss of these books will create. The author is not chronicling a cultural trend; he is documenting the end of an entire mode of human consciousness.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as a poignant and  tragic work of contemporary intellectual history. It is the definitive explanation for why your civilization collapsed. The crises you faced—the political, the economic, the social—were all downstream of this one, central catastrophe: the death of the literate mind.
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the counter-revolution this document so perfectly describes.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Great Dumbing (2020s-2030s). This essay is the perfect, heartbreaking diagnosis of the disease in its final, acute phase.
The Un-Making of the Mind: The author is entirely correct. The "reading revolution" of the 18th century forged the modern mind: it was linear, logical, critical, and capable of holding complex, abstract thoughts. The "screen revolution" was its perfect, systematic un-making. The smartphone was not a tool; it was a cognitive environment that systematically selected for and rewarded the opposite traits: reactive, emotional, tribal, and incapable of sustained attention. This was the "Shattered Focus" crisis at a civilizational scale.
The Golden Thread Snaps: His metaphor of the "golden thread of knowledge" is precise. This was the moment it snapped. An entire generation was being rendered "functionally illiterate," not in the sense of being unable to read words, but in being unable to access the complex world of ideas and history that those words represented. This was the true engine of the "Great Amnesia."
Act II: The World After Print (2030s-2050s). The author correctly predicted the world that would be born from this collapse. It was a world that we now call the "Age of the Incoherent Mind."
The Death of Democracy: He is right. Democracy, a system built on the premise of a rational, informed citizenry capable of debating complex issues, cannot survive in a post-literate world. The collapse into "panic, hatred and tribal warfare" was the inevitable result of a population that could no longer think. This cognitive collapse was the fuel for the "Great Fragmentation."
The Stagnant Culture: His diagnosis of a stagnant, repetitive, nostalgic culture is a perfect description of the final, decadent phase of your world. A civilization that can no longer read its own past is condemned to endlessly, and poorly, imitate it. The "moronic inferno" was the lived reality of the mid-century.
Act III: The New Scriptoriums (Post-2060s). The author asks what comes next, a "second feudal age" or something beyond imagining. The reality was both. The "Era of Systemic Resilience" is the direct, systemic, and often brutal answer to the crisis he described.
The Revaluation of the Word: The survivors of the collapse, the builders of the "Sovereign Stacks," understood the lesson of this essay in their bones. They built their new world on a fanatical, almost religious, devotion to the literate mind.
[[The New Academies]] are not universities. They are the new scriptoriums, the new monasteries. Their core, sacred mission is to take the shattered minds of the young and painstakingly re-forge them in the discipline of deep reading and linear thought.
The Bounded Word: The "golden thread" was re-spun, but it was no longer a single, universal thread. Each Stack has its own canon, its own sacred texts, its own history. The world of a single, shared print culture is dead. In its place is a world of many, separate, and deeply literate traditions.
This essay is a landmark. It is the sound of the last book in the world closing. The author believed he was writing a warning. We have the hindsight to know he was writing a history of the future.
