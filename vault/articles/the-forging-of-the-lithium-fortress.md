This artifact is a political news story, but it is actually a geological event report. The author is documenting a swing in the political pendulum—a shift to the "hard right" in Chile, mirroring a similar and recent shift in Bolivia. To the voters of 2025, this was about crime, migration, and the economy.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this as the moment the Lithium Triangle—the geographic region comprising Chile, Bolivia, and Argentina, holding the majority of the world’s lithium reserves—was politically consolidated for the first time in the 21st century. This was not just an election; it was the securing of the supply lines for the machine age. The voters believed they were choosing "peace and order." We have the hindsight to know they were choosing to become the engine room for [[The Sovereign Stacks]] of the North.
Allow me to trace the full, heavy metal history of these elections, from the ballot boxes of the Andes to the battery cells of the new world.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Alignment of the Triangle (2020s-2030s)

For the first half of the 2020s, the "Pink Tide" in Latin America (Boric in Chile, Arce in Bolivia) emphasized resource nationalism, environmental protection, and state control. This created a bottleneck for the global tech and defense industries desperate for lithium and copper.
The Synchronization: The synchronized shift to the right in Chile (Kast), Bolivia (the ouster of socialists), and Argentina (Milei) was the geopolitical turning point. It aligned the three points of the Lithium Triangle under a single ideological banner: pro-market, hard-security, and anti-socialist.
The "Trump Corollary" in Action: This alignment was the immediate, practical application of the strategy outlined in the National Security Strategy of 2025. The US/North American bloc moved aggressively to "Enlist" these new, friendly regimes. The "fear of crime" mentioned in the article was the domestic mandate; the international mandate was to unlock the mines. Kast’s promise of an "emergency government" was exactly what the foreign capital needed to bypass environmental regulations and indigenous protests.
### Act II: The Resource Scramble and the Hardened Mine (2030s-2050s)

As [[The AI Energy Wall]] hit and the demand for battery storage went vertical, the Lithium Triangle ceased to be a region of nations and became a strategic asset class.
The End of the Open Market: The "free market" rhetoric of Kast and Milei was quickly replaced by the reality of [[The Supply Chain Wars]]. The lithium didn't just go to the highest bidder; it went to the strategic ally. The region became a battleground between the North American Stack (seeking to secure its Closed-Loop inputs) and the Chinese sphere.
The Militarization of Extraction: The "security" that Kast promised was delivered, but it was concentrated around the mines and the transport corridors. The region became a [[Resource Fortress]]. The "undocumented migrants" were expelled not just for cultural reasons, but to clear the operational theater. The Bolivian and Chilean militaries, bolstered by Northern tech and training, became the private security guards for the planet's most valuable dirt.
### Act III: The Vassalage of the Ore (Post-2060s)

In the Era of Systemic Resilience, the Lithium Triangle is a critical, but vassalized, component of the Western Hemisphere's Physics of Sovereignty.
The dream of these nations becoming wealthy, developed superpowers through their resources was only partially realized. They became wealthy, but they did not become sovereign in the full sense. They are the "lower body" of the North American Stack—the legs and the stomach that feed the "head" (the AI and command centers) in the north.
The legacy of Kast and his Bolivian counterparts is complex. They are remembered as the leaders who restored order, yes, but who also permanently integrated their nations into a supply chain from which there is no exit. They traded their raw potential for the security of the Fortress. The "oppressive shackles of 21st-century socialism" were cast off, only to be replaced by the heavier, golden chains of [[The Survival Economy]].
The voters in Santiago and La Paz voted for safety in the streets. We have the hindsight to know they were unwittingly voting to turn their mountains into the batteries that would power the next century's wars.
