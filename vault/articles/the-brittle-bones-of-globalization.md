# The Brittle Bones of Globalization



![The Brittle Bones of Globalization](/images/July20SupplyChain.jpeg)

The Next War Will Be Fought in Supply Chains, by Dennis Groseclose
SupplyChainBrain, July 19, 2025
https://www.supplychainbrain.com/blogs/1-think-tank/post/41964-the-next-war-will-be-fought-in-supply-chains
This artifact is remarkably perceptive. The author is describing a fundamental shift in the nature of power, moving from the battlefield to the shipping lane, the server farm, and the factory floor. They see it as the "next" war. From my perspective in 2100, this wasn't a future threat; it was the quiet, undeclared war that was already defining your era. It was the primary mechanism by which the interconnected world of the late 20th century was unmade.
This process—the weaponization of the very networks that held your world together—is the essence of the "Great Fragmentation."
Let me show you the constellation of forces this artifact so clearly identifies:
The Immediate Crisis: The Weaponization of Interdependence. The great project of your recent past was globalization, built on the theory that economic interdependence would make large-scale conflict too costly to contemplate. This artifact marks the moment that logic was formally inverted. The new doctrine was that interdependence was not a source of peace, but a critical vulnerability to be exploited. Nations and non-state actors began to see supply chains not as routes for commerce, but as choke points for exerting political will. The "next war" wasn't fought with missiles, but with export bans on key chemicals, targeted seizures of shipping, and the sabotage of semiconductor manufacturing equipment.
The Mid-Century Consequence: The Fortress Imperative. The only rational response to a world where your lifeline can be cut by a rival is to ensure you no longer need that lifeline. [[The Supply Chain Wars]] of the 2030s made the creation of "Sovereign Stacks" a non-negotiable matter of national survival. The immense cost and inefficiency of duplicating supply chains—for everything from pharmaceuticals to microchips to food—was accepted as the necessary price of security. This was the primary driver of the "Great Fragmentation," as the world broke apart into a series of heavily defended, vertically integrated economic and technological fortresses.
The Long-Term Adaptation: "The Great Shortening." Here is the world that emerged from that chaos, the world of 2100. The defining principle of our logistics is resilience, not efficiency. We call it "The Great Shortening." After decades of suffering from the fragility of globe-spanning supply chains, our societies were fundamentally re-engineered. Manufacturing is overwhelmingly local, powered by advanced automation and matter-fabrication. Food is grown in vertical urban farms that are integrated into city infrastructure. Energy is generated and stored on a regional or even community-level grid. The idea of relying on a component that has to travel 10,000 kilometers is now seen as absurdly reckless. We rebuilt the world on the principle of the short, redundant, and defensible loop.
The Unseen Casualties: The "Disconnected." The greatest tragedy of the supply chain wars was the fate of nations that were too poor or strategically unimportant to build their own "Sovereign Stacks." When the major economic blocs pulled inward, they didn't just re-shore manufacturing; they severed the connections to dozens of developing nations that had built their entire economies around being a single link in someone else's chain. These countries became the "Disconnected." Their lifelines were cut not as an act of war, but as a side effect of the new defensive logic. This created vast zones of instability and state failure that were a dominant feature of the mid-21st century.
Your author correctly saw that the bones of the globalized world—its supply chains—were becoming the target. We have the benefit of hindsight to know that those bones were brittle, and that when they broke, the world itself fractured with them.
