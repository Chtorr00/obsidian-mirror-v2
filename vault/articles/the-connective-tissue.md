# The Connective Tissue



![The Connective Tissue](/images/July12ConnectiveTissue.jpeg)

Our crisis is not loneliness but human beings becoming invisible, by Allison Pugh
Aeon, July 12, 2025
https://aeon.co/essays/our-crisis-is-not-loneliness-but-human-beings-becoming-invisible
Thank you for this artifact. The author has put her finger on a distinction of immense historical importance. Your era was obsessed with the term "loneliness." From my perspective, this was a misdiagnosis. Loneliness, the subjective feeling of lacking connection, was merely a symptom. The underlying disease, as the author so clearly articulates, was something deeper: a crisis of invisibility.  It was the sense of being unseen, unheard, and unvalued as a unique human being—of being rendered a mere abstraction by the very systems you were building.
This feeling of being a ghost in your own life was one of the most powerful and underestimated forces of the late 2020s. It was the quiet, pervasive erosion of the connective tissue that holds a society together.
Let me show you the constellation of forces that made this crisis of invisibility so acute:
The Transactional Gaze: The author identifies how work, particularly in the gig economy, was becoming "depersonalised." People were becoming vending machines, mere functions in an automated system. This was not limited to work. Social media, with its logic of likes and shares, trained you to see others (and yourselves) as profiles to be consumed, not as people to be known. Every interaction, from commerce to friendship, was being subtly redesigned to be as efficient and frictionless as possible, stripping away the "unnecessary" human complexities where true recognition happens.
The Illusion of Connection: Your digital networks created a paradox: you were more connected than ever, yet you felt more invisible. They gave you an audience, but not a witness. You could broadcast your thoughts to the world, but the feeling of being truly seen by a trusted few became increasingly rare. This digital static drowned out the slower, more demanding work of genuine community, where visibility is earned through presence, patience, and mutual obligation.
The Loss of Legibility: As AI and big data systems became more integrated into governance and commerce, a new kind of invisibility emerged. The systems that made decisions about your life—your credit score, your job prospects, your health insurance—were increasingly complex and opaque. Individuals began to feel they were being judged by algorithms they couldn't understand and couldn't appeal to. They were no longer visible to power as citizens or people, but merely as data points. This fueled a profound sense of powerlessness that fed directly into the "Age of Institutional Exhaustion."
The Hunger for Recognition: It is a fundamental human need to be seen. When your society's primary systems—economic, social, and political—begin to make people invisible, they will seek that recognition elsewhere, and often in destructive ways. This hunger for visibility fueled the extreme political polarization and "Neotribalism" of the 2030s. Finding a tribe, any tribe, that would finally see you and validate your experience became more important than any shared national or universal identity. The crisis of invisibility was the fertile ground in which the "Great Fragmentation" took root.
Your author was writing an essay on sociology. From my perspective, she was writing a critical, if unwitting, intelligence briefing on the single greatest vulnerability of your social order. The question wasn't how to cure loneliness, but whether a society that makes its own people invisible can survive.
