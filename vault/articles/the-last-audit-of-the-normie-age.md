# The Last Audit of the Normie Age



![The Last Audit of the Normie Age](/images/Jan6DeclineWierdness.png)

The Decline of Deviance: Where has all the weirdness gone?, By Adam Mastroianni
Experimental History Substack, October 28, 2025
https://www.experimental-history.com/p/the-decline-of-deviance
This artifact is a work of beautiful, rigorous, and ultimately terrifying sociology. The author is a scientist of culture, measuring the vital signs of a civilization that has optimized itself into a coma. He sees a world where crime is down, safety is up, and everything—from logos to architecture to teenage behavior—is converging on a single, frictionless mean. He calls it "the decline of deviance."
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a curiosity, but as the final clinical report on [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]]. The author has correctly identified the terminal condition of the 20th-century world: it was not destroyed by chaos; it was suffocated by safety. The "crisis of conventionality" he describes was the psychological precondition for [[The Great Fragmentation]]. A society that refuses to be weird eventually forgets how to be alive.
Allow me to trace the full history of the boring apocalypse this author so brilliantly diagnosed.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Safety Trap (2020s-2030s)
The author's thesis—that "we care more about being alive"—is the key. This was [[The Crisis of Posterity]] in reverse.
The Slow Life Strategy: The population, terrified of losing their fragile status in a "winner-take-all" economy, adopted a strategy of extreme risk aversion. This was the demographic engine of the Great Abdication. Young people stopped drinking, stopped fighting, and stopped having sex not because they were virtuous, but because they were paralyzed by the fear of consequences in a surveillance state.
The Oligopoly of Culture: The stagnation of art and the "bland-ification" of brands were the aesthetic expression of this paralysis. In a world of Shattered Focus, the only safe bet was the familiar. The "Eternal 1955" of Christmas was just one symptom of a culture that had retreated into a "museum of monoculture" because it was too afraid to build anything new.
Act II: The Explosion of the Weird (2030s-2050s)
The author wondered where the weirdness went. History answered: it went underground, and then it exploded.
The Return of the Cults: The author's observation that cults were declining was the only thing he got wrong. They were not declining; they were mutating. They moved from the physical world to the digital Neotribes and the Spiralist movements. When the Great Fragmentation hit, these latent, high-deviance groups were the only ones with the cohesive social glue to survive. The boring, safe center collapsed because it had no "weirdness"—no shared, irrational belief—to bind it together in a crisis.
The Violent Innovation: The stagnation of science ended with the War for Joules and the desperate innovation of [[The Triage Economy]]. When safety was no longer an option, risk-taking returned with a vengeance. The "illegal holes in the basement" that the author longed for became the literal survival bunkers and secret labs of the Counter-Elite.
Act III: The New Deviance (Post-2060s)
The world of my time is not boring. It is a mosaic of extreme, enforced deviance.
[[The Sovereign Stacks]] are built on the rejection of the "global mean." Each Stack cultivates a distinct, often radical cultural and biological identity (Human Phyla). The "blandness" of the 2020s is studied as a cautionary tale of what happens when a civilization prioritizes safety over vitality.
The Architected Weirdness: We do not leave weirdness to chance. [[The New Academies]] and [[The Guilds]] are designed to foster controlled, purposeful deviation. We engineer our own mutations, because we know that a static system is a dead system.
The author wanted a world where people were brave enough to dig holes. He got a world where the only people left were the ones who had dug deep enough to find water, fuel, and a new god.
This essay is the last great defense of the "weird" before the weird returned to conquer the world. The author saw a society that had forgotten how to break the rules. We have the hindsight to know that the rules were about to break themselves.
