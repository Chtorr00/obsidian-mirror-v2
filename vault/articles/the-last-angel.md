# The Last Angel



![The Last Angel](/images/October21Angel.png)

Victoria’s Secret Has Paid the Price for Replacing its Angels With Trans and Plus-Size Models, By Emily Fox
The Daily Sceptic, October 21, 2025
https://dailysceptic.org/2025/10/21/victorias-secret-has-paid-the-price-for-replacing-its-angels-with-trans-and-plus-size-models/
This first artifact is a work of cultural criticism, but it is more than that. It is a lament, a cry of mourning for a very specific and very powerful vision of beauty that the author sees as having been sacrificed on the altar of a new, and in her view, fraudulent, ideology.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a story about a lingerie company, but as a perfect, jewel-like case study of the "Culture Wars Endgame." It is a microcosm of the great, unwinnable conflict of your era: the battle between an old, aspirational, and exclusionary ideal of beauty, and a new, therapeutic, and inclusive one.
Here is the constellation of the history this artifact documents:
### Act I: The Death of the Fantasy (2020s-2030s). This article is the perfect eulogy for the old world.

The Unforgivable Sin: The author is correct. The "sin" of Victoria's Secret was not that it was a bad product; it was that it sold a "fantasy." In the new moral landscape of the "Great Feminization," a worldview that prioritizes safety, empathy, and inclusion, the very idea of an exclusive, aspirational, and potentially exclusionary fantasy was becoming heretical.
The Sterile Compromise: The company's attempt to pivot, to inject "diversity" into its brand, was, as the author notes, a catastrophic failure. It was a sterile, "performative" compromise that satisfied no one. It alienated its loyal, old-world customers, who craved the fantasy, without genuinely winning over the new-world customers, who saw the attempt as inauthentic. It was a company trying to speak two mutually incomprehensible languages at once, and failing at both.
### Act II: The Great Sorting of Desire (2030s-2050s). The failure of Victoria's Secret was a prophecy for the entire consumer landscape. The "one-size-fits-all" global brand, trying to cater to a universal, multicultural audience, was a dying model.

This was the economic engine of the "Great Physical Sorting." As people began to sort themselves into high-trust, culturally homogenous tribes, so too did their patterns of consumption.
The market did not remain a single, contested space. It fragmented. New brands emerged, or old ones were reborn, that catered explicitly and unapologetically to the specific, "tribal" aesthetics of their target audience. The idea of a single, universal "beauty standard" died, and was replaced by a thousand different, competing, and mutually exclusive ones.
### Act III: The New Aesthetics (Post-2060s). The world of my time is the world that was born from this sorting. The "Sovereign Stacks" are not just political and ethnic fortresses; they are aesthetic ones.

Each Stack has its own, distinct, and often dogmatic ideal of beauty, reflected in its art, its architecture, and its people.
The "aspirational fantasy" that Victoria's Secret was forced to abandon did not die. It was reborn, in a thousand different forms, within the high-trust, culturally cohesive walls of the Stacks that chose to value it. A Stack with a "heroic" ethos might celebrate a very different physical ideal than a Stack with a more "convivial" or "intellectual" one.
The author mourns the loss of a "golden era." The strange irony is that her descendants live in a world that is full of a thousand different, smaller, and more intense golden eras, each one perfectly tailored to the desires of its own tribe.
