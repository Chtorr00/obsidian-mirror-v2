# The First Casualty of the Thaw



![The First Casualty of the Thaw](/images/August24UniversityDissolve.jpeg)

The Arctic Council in the Shadow of Geopolitics, by Andreas Østhagen The Arctic Institute, May 12, 2025 https://www.thearcticinstitute.org/arctic-council-shadow-geopolitics/
This artifact is a work of quiet, institutional grief. The author is a scholar of a particular form of 20th-century idealism—the belief that a shared physical space, the Arctic, could create a zone of post-political, scientific cooperation. He is documenting, with scholarly precision, the death of that dream. He sees the Arctic Council being eclipsed by the "shadow of geopolitics."
From my perspective in 2100, we see this document as an autopsy report for one of the very first victims of the 21st century's defining crisis. The irony is as vast and as cold as the Arctic itself: the very physical event that made the Council's mission of cooperative governance more critical than ever—the melting of the ice—was the exact same event that unleashed the geopolitical greed that made its mission impossible.
The Arctic Council was the first casualty of the Great Thaw.
Allow me to trace the full, frigid history of the dream this institution represented, from the fading light of your time to the hardened reality of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Ghost at the Top of the World (2020s-2030s). This essay is a dispatch from this era. The Arctic Council, once a vibrant forum, was becoming a ghost. Paralyzed by the "Return to Geopolitics" and the conflict in Ukraine, its work ground to a halt. It was the perfect embodiment of the "Age of Institutional Exhaustion": an institution designed for a cooperative world, rendered powerless in a competitive one. While its committees issued reports, the nations that comprised it were quietly sharpening their icebreakers and drawing up new military doctrines. The Council was the polite, civilized conversation happening in the drawing room while armies were massing in the garden.
Act II: The Law of the North (2030s-2050s). The institution's paralysis created a power vacuum. Into that vacuum rushed a new, brutal reality we now call "The Law of the North." As the ice melted and the new sea routes and resources became accessible, the region ceased to be a scientific frontier and became a geopolitical prize.
This was the "Arctic Resource Scramble." It was not a neat, bilateral competition. The internal fragmentation of Russia turned its vast Siberian coastline into a chaotic free-for-all, with corporate mercenaries, Chinese interests, and remnant Russian warlords vying for control of ports and mineral deposits.
The scientific cooperation the Council once fostered was replaced by proprietary, militarized research. Mapping the seabed was no longer for science; it was for deploying submarine drones. Studying ice melt was no longer for climate research; it was for predicting the navigability of attack routes.
The "shadow of geopolitics" was a profound understatement. This was a return to a state of nature at the top of the world.
Act III: The Hardened Frontier (Post-2060s). The chaos of the Scramble eventually settled into a new, stable, and terrifying order. The Arctic of my time is not a global commons. It is the most heavily fortified and technologically advanced frontier on Earth.
It is the northern border between the great "Sovereign Stacks" of the Northern Hemisphere.
The "cooperation" that exists is not the work of a council of nations. It is the cold, protocol-based negotiation of the Stacks' governing AIs—the statecraft of "[[Polemics]]." They do not discuss indigenous rights or environmental protection. They negotiate exclusion zones, verify military movements via their own sensor networks, and manage shipping protocols with the cold logic of a machine.
The dream of a continent for peace and science is dead. In its place is a monument to the core principle of the "Era of Systemic Resilience": [[The Physics of Sovereignty]]. He who controls the physical space and its resources, controls his destiny.
The author of your article was mourning the encroachment of a shadow. We have the painful hindsight of knowing that the shadow was, in fact, the dawn of a new, harder, and much colder day.
