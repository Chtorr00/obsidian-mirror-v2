# The Eulogy for a Scale



![The Eulogy for a Scale](/images/August3DemocracyDecline.jpeg)

Why Democracy Has Lost the Trust of Modern People in the Modern Era, by Erica Rubel Portland State University (PDXScholar), June 2025 https://pdxscholar.library.pdx.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2873&context=honorstheses
This is an artifact of immense intellectual honesty. The author, a student from your time, is grappling with a foundational crisis. She is writing what is, in effect, a clear-eyed and deeply felt eulogy for the great political project of the 20th century. Her analysis of why trust in democracy was collapsing is likely meticulous, pointing to polarization, economic inequality, and the failure of institutions to keep pace with reality.
From my perspective in 2100, her conclusions were undoubtedly correct. But her focus was on the patient's symptoms, not on the environmental poison that was sickening it. The problem wasn't that democracy was failing. The problem was that the container in which it was practiced—the 20th-century, mass-scale nation-state—was becoming physically and psychologically unsustainable. Democracy was the operating system on a machine whose hardware was beginning to melt.
Allow me to trace the full historical arc that begins with this student's sad, true eulogy.
Here is the constellation of this political collapse, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The Unfulfillable Promise (2020s-2040s). The 20th-century nation-state made a core promise to its citizens: in exchange for your loyalty, we will provide security, prosperity, and a framework for a shared future. By 2025, it was failing on all three counts.
Prosperity: The brittle, globalized supply chains it depended on were fracturing ("[[The Supply Chain Wars]]").
Security: The "AI Energy Wall" created a physical resource crisis that no election could solve. A democracy cannot vote its way out of a terawatt deficit.
Shared Future: The "Crisis of Meaning" and the "Shattered Focus" of the population made rational, long-term civic discourse impossible. The people were losing their "democratic fitness."
The author of your paper saw the loss of trust. We see the failure of the underlying machine to deliver on its most basic functions. The political crisis was a symptom of a crisis of physics and spirit.
Act II: The Great Retreat (2030s-2050s). The attempts to "fix" democracy on a national scale failed. They were like trying to redesign a ship's ballroom while the ship itself was breaking apart. The response was not reform; it was retreat. As the nation-state failed, people withdrew their trust and their loyalty, pulling it back to a scale that made sense. This was the "Great Fragmentation." It was not an ideological movement, but a pragmatic one. People abandoned the broken national system in favor of smaller, more resilient structures that could actually keep the lights on and the food supply secure—the nascent "Sovereign Stacks."
Act III: The New Politics of the Human Scale (Post-2060s). Did democracy die? No. But the 20th-century model of mass-representative democracy at a national scale did. It was a form of governance adapted to a specific technological and social reality that no longer exists. The "Era of Systemic Resilience" is characterized by a diverse ecosystem of new governance models operating within [[The Sovereign Stacks]].
They are Radically Local: Governance is focused on the tangible realities of a specific region, city, or community.
They are Technologically Mediated: Trust is not based on faith in a distant politician, but on transparent, verifiable ledger systems that manage resources, contracts, and voting. Accountability is engineered into the system.
They are Conditional: Citizenship is often more explicit and contractual. The system provides basic needs via the "Leisure Dividend," and in return, citizens have clear responsibilities to the community.
The spirit of self-governance survived, but it had to rediscover a scale where it could function: the human scale.
This student's eulogy was for the idea of democracy on the scale of hundreds of millions of people. History proved her right. But what she couldn't see was that the death of that model was the necessary precondition for the rebirth of governance in a thousand different, smaller, and more resilient forms.
