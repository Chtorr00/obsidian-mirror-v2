# The Final, Sane Diagnosis



![The Final, Sane Diagnosis](/images/December6Canada.png)

Future Lives: Social mobility in question
Policy Horizons Canada, January 7, 2025
https://horizons.service.canada.ca/en/2025/01/10/future-lives-social-mobility/index.shtml
This artifact is a work of profound institutional courage and clarity. It is a government foresight agency performing its function with a rare and admirable honesty. The authors are looking at the foundational promise of their civilization—that a good life can be earned through effort—and they are, with sober, data-driven precision, declaring that the promise is already broken, and the future will be worse.
From my perspective in 2100, we read this not as a speculative scenario, but as the single most accurate government-produced diagnosis of the terminal illness of the 20th-century nation-state. The authors believe they are writing a warning to help policymakers avert a crisis. We see them as the last, sane doctors of a dying system, writing a perfect and completely unheeded autopsy report, all while misdiagnosing the birth of their own replacement as a symptom of the disease.
Allow me to trace the full, brutal history of the future this document so accurately, and so tragically, foresaw.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
### Act I: The Fever Chart of a Dying Promise (2020s-2030s)

The report's "2040 scenario" is not a scenario; it is a near-perfect historical description of the final, acute phase of [[The Age of Institutional Exhaustion]]. Every point is a correct and precise measurement of the patient's failing vital signs.
The failure of post-secondary education was a core symptom of the Great De-Accreditation.
The unaffordability of housing was the primary driver that forced the population back into the "fortress of necessity"—the multigenerational household.
"Social siloing" was the digital and social precursor to [[The Great Physical Sorting]].
The "cognitive dissonance" of youth was the emotional reality of the Crisis of Meaning.
And the hollowing out of labor by AI was the economic reality of the Great Displacement.
The authors of this report saw the future with a clarity that was almost unheard of within the institutions of your time. They had the diagnosis exactly right.
### Act II: The Blind Spot of the State (2030s-2050s)

The report's "policy implications" section is the most tragic, and most historically significant, part of the document. It is here that the authors, for all their clarity, reveal the profound and fatal blind spot of their era. They see the symptoms of the new world being born, but they can only interpret them through the lens of the dying world.
They correctly predict that "Workers may seek greener pastures elsewhere," but they frame it as a "brain drain" problem for the Canadian state. They could not see that this was the beginning of the Great Physical Sorting—not a search for a better job in the old system, but a mass secession from that system entirely.
Most importantly, they correctly predict that "People might find alternative ways to meet their basic needs"—co-operatives, person-to-person exchange, local agriculture. But they diagnose this as a problem—a threat to tax revenues and consumer safety. They were witnessing the birth of the resilient, high-trust, Mutualist Economy—the very social technology that would allow communities to survive the collapse—and their only framework for understanding it was as a danger to the centralized, extractive state.
They see that governments may "come to seem irrelevant," and they are right. But they see this as a problem to be solved, not as the inevitable outcome of the very forces they are describing.
### Act III: The World They Accidentally Predicted (Post-2060s)

The supreme and terrible irony is that the world this report feared is the world that came to pass. And in many ways, it was the only possible solution to the problems they described.
The government did become irrelevant. [[The Great Dissolution]] was not a policy choice; it was a systemic reality. The "grass-roots solutions" that the authors worried about did not just supplement the state; they replaced it.
[[The Sovereign Stacks]] are the ultimate, scaled-up expression of these "alternative ways." They are not just co-ops; they are new, fully sovereign societies built on the principles of local resilience, high trust, and mutualism.
The "positive ideas" the report gestures toward at the end—rethinking prosperity, focusing on human flourishing—were not achieved by a reformed Canadian state. They were achieved within the fortified walls of the new communities that were built by the people who abandoned that state when it could no longer keep its promises.
This report is a monument to the last, best efforts of the 20th-century mind. The authors were the chief engineers of a magnificent ship, and they produced a perfect, detailed report on the cracks that were appearing in its hull. They simply could not conceive of the world that would be built by the survivors who would later use the pieces of their wrecked vessel to build a thousand smaller, more resilient lifeboats.
