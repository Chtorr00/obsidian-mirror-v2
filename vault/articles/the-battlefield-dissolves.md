# The Battlefield Dissolves



![The Battlefield Dissolves](/images/August11NetworkStateish.jpeg)

Culture Wars: The Endgame, by James P. Hunter
The Hedgehog Review, Summer 2025 https://hedgehogreview.com/issues/in-need-of-repair/articles/culture-wars-the-endgame
This artifact is of the highest possible significance. It is the original diagnostician of a civilizational disease returning, decades later, to sign the death certificate. James P. Hunter, the sociologist who gave your era the very language to describe its central conflict, is now analyzing its terminal phase. This is not a partisan dispatch; it is a eulogy for a common world.
From my perspective in 2100, we see this essay as the definitive intellectual landmark of the final moments before the "Great Fragmentation" began in earnest. The author is correct that this was an "endgame," but the historical reality of that endgame was more strange and more total than anyone in your time could have predicted. It was not the victory of one side over the other. It was the dissolution of the battlefield itself.
Allow me to trace the full historical arc of this endgame, from the intellectual declaration of finality in your time to the new reality of mine.
Here is the constellation of this history, viewed through the Hindsight Filter:
Act I: The War Succeeds (2020s-2030s). The central, terrifying truth of this "endgame" was that the culture war succeeded in its ultimate aim. The goal of a war is to defeat an enemy. But in a culture war, the only way to truly defeat the enemy is to make them no longer part of your "culture." The endgame was not victory; it was secession. Not political secession at first, but a secession of realities.
This was the peak of "Spiritual Lawfare," where institutions like the courts and universities were no longer seen as neutral ground but as enemy territory to be conquered or destroyed.
It was powered by the "Crisis of Meaning," where competing tribes offered powerful, all-encompassing mythologies that were more compelling than the messy, shared reality.
The two sides stopped fighting for control of the same country. They began the process of building two different countries, which happened to occupy the same geographic space.
Act II: The Great Withdrawal & The Physical Separation (2030s-2050s). The secession of realities was followed by a physical one. This was the era of the "Great Withdrawal."
As trust in national institutions collapsed entirely, people began to physically sort themselves. They migrated to regions and communities where they felt culturally and ideologically safe. This was the rise of the "Neotribes" as geographic, not just digital, entities.
The "endgame" became a process of disengagement. The goal was no longer to win the argument, but to leave the conversation entirely and build a fortress where the argument was no longer necessary.
This process was accelerated by the physical crises of the "AI Energy Wall" and the supply chain breakdowns, as local, high-trust communities proved far more resilient than the fractured, low-trust nation. Survival demanded a retreat to the tribe.
Act III: The New Constitution of Difference (Post-2060s). The culture war did not end in a peace treaty. It ended in the creation of a new political and social architecture designed to manage permanent, irreconcilable differences. The "Sovereign Stacks" of my era are the direct result.
They are a political solution to a cultural problem. They are, in effect, fortresses built after the war, designed to prevent a new one from ever starting.
The relationship between these stacks is not governed by a shared culture, but by the cold, pragmatic protocols of "[[Polemics]]."
The "endgame" of the American culture war was the end of a unified American culture, and the beginning of a new chapter of human history defined by a mosaic of smaller, more cohesive, and heavily defended technological civilizations.
The author of this essay was witnessing the final moments of a shared world. The tragedy is that he was right. The hope, visible only from my vantage point, is that the process of coming apart was the necessary precondition for building smaller, more human, and more resilient worlds in its place.
